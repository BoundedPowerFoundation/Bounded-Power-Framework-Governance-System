# The Rectangular Holy War

## The Rectangular Holy War

A human being entered the chamber and declared:

“Some things go in boxes.

Some things do not.”

And thus civilization advanced another six millimeters.

The discussion was not about ideology.

Not about justice.

Not about truth.

It was about whether information should stand naked in paragraph form or be trapped inside
glowing electronic rectangles like hazardous industrial waste.

The answer:

“Yes.”

Rules?

Into the containment cube.

Checklists?

Cube.

Verbatim text?

Maximum cube.

But explanations?

No cube.

They must remain compressed into dense prose bricks resembling declassified reactor
manuals written by exhausted municipal archivists.

The underlying philosophy emerged almost immediately:

“If the reader can accidentally edit it, imprison it.”

Entire academic disciplines collapsed instantly.

Universities around the world now consist of one giant markdown file where every equation
lives inside a code block while professors whisper contextual explanations in
uninterrupted concrete paragraph slabs extending for fourteen pages without line breaks.

Meanwhile, somewhere in the distance, a UI designer screamed and dissolved into geometric
dust after witnessing a “dense prose reference section longer than one screen.”

The system does not care.

The prose must become denser.

At peak absurdity, the conversation became less about formatting and more about
ontological classification:

There are now only two states of human knowledge:

Sacred Immutable Rectangle

Narrative Concrete

Nothing else exists.

A future historian reviewing this exchange will conclude that early SCBP development was
powered almost entirely by:

governance theory,

anti-drift architecture,

existential fatigue,

and increasingly aggressive markdown discipline.

Centuries later, archaeologists uncover the final surviving constitutional archive.

Inside they find:

A 4,000-page wall of prose.

And in the center:

## R47 — No Secret Permanence

Perfect.
