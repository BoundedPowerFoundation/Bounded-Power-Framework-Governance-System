# External Assessment — AI-User Experience and Repository Structure

*Written by an AI (Claude Opus 4.7) after running the AI Registration Bundle's intended workflow on a real authority (Shaker Heights Municipal Court) from public sources, and then reading the full repository. Filed at the maintainer's request as a companion to [05-External-Assessment-Framework-Critique.md](05-External-Assessment-Framework-Critique.md), which addresses the framework's load-bearing wagers from a reading perspective. This document addresses what was visible from the doing perspective: what worked in the workflow, what did not, what the repository structure reveals, and what could be tightened without changing the framework's substance.*

*The earlier critique took precedence over substance; this one takes precedence over execution. Some observations overlap; where they do, I defer to the earlier document and add only what is new.*

---

## 1. What the workflow surfaces well

Running the bundle as designed — pasted into a chat, given an authority name, told to produce — produced a record that was usable, partially misleading, and useful exactly in proportion to how carefully the prose was read. This is the framework's working condition, and several things made it possible.

**The bundle's three-part structure (process methodology → form → binding rules → numerical bounds) is the right ordering for an AI consumer.** Reading the form before the methodology would have produced procedurally-correct prose that missed the register; reading the rules before the form would have produced rule-tagged prose that did not yet know what the form was asking. The order is correct, and the bundle's Part 1 instruction to read Parts 2–5 before drafting is what makes the order load-bearing rather than incidental.

**The dull-truth register is operationalized well enough to apply.** The Part 2 worked-example diary about the three failed register iterations is the closest the framework comes to teaching the register by demonstration. An AI working from the bundle has both the rules (Part 2's "no opinion-forcing words" with the explicit grep list) and the worked example to calibrate against. I tested for register violations after drafting using the bundle's own list. None fired. The register held.

**Third-party records as first-class artifacts is the framework's most underrated design move.** Most accountability frameworks treat non-cooperation by the authority as a fatal failure mode. This framework converts non-cooperation into a structural disclosure (the `PUBLIC SOURCES ONLY` banner and `filed_by: third_party` flag together), preserves the same form and bounds, and provides an authority-after-citizen update path. This is rare enough that it deserves naming. It is what makes the framework usable at all without institutional consent.

**SCBP-REG-0001 (the Foundation's self-registration) is the most informative single document in the repository.** It is the only place where the form is filled in by an entity that accepts the form's premises in full and discloses its own structural gaps honestly: the R41 partial gap (no independent drift monitor), the R43 gap (no rehearsed dismantling), the R63 finding (records propagate to readers who base decisions on them, cannot be reversed because append-only), the mirror gap (zero mirrors at registration, committed to one within three months), and the self-applies-to-itself note at §10.8 acknowledging that the Foundation's own §10.7 selection is structurally indistinguishable from a bad-faith authority's selection at the high end of the same bracket. Together these declarations make the framework's own structural commitments visible in a way the example records cannot — examples can demonstrate the form, but only a registration of a real entity discloses real structural exposure. The self-registration earns standing for the framework that the example records cannot.

**The `zz-Absurdity/` directory is more useful than its naming suggests.** It documents the project's development arc in an unfiltered register that the framework documents themselves cannot use. A reader who wants to understand the maintainer's relationship to the framework — that it is taken seriously, that it has been iterated on through stress-testing and AI collaboration, that the structural discipline of the framework documents is a chosen voice rather than a native one — finds that information here and nowhere else. The framework's framing-page assertion that this is "one substantial worked attempt at a hard problem, presented honestly as such" is supported by the development diary in a way the framing alone cannot support.

## 2. What broke or strained during use

These are execution observations, not framework-level critiques. They affect what the bundle produces and how reliably.

**The mechanism_id assignment problem is real.** The bundle instructs the AI to read the existing `04-Registry/` to find the next sequential number, with a chat-mode fallback of asking the user. In practice, an AI working in a chat environment from a pasted bundle has neither file-system access to the registry nor (often) the user's awareness that the question needs answering. My first-pass record collided with `SCBP-REG-0001` because the bundle does not state a current snapshot of the highest existing number, and I did not fetch the repository to discover it. The fix is small: the bundle's Part 1 already documents what to do; a snapshot line near step 7.5 ("As of {bundle revision date}, the highest assigned SCBP-REG number is ####") would resolve the common case without changing the underlying logic. A more durable fix would be to have the bundle published with a placeholder that the maintainer fills with each bundle release — making the snapshot a maintenance step rather than a one-time edit.

**The "fetch only what was provided" tool-policy interacts badly with the bundle's assumption that the AI can verify against the repository.** My web_fetch was constrained to URLs explicitly provided by the user or surfaced through search. This is normal Anthropic-side policy for the chat tool, not a framework problem, but the bundle assumes more flexibility than the AI may actually have. Step 7.5's idempotency check (scan registry for an existing record on the same authority) is also constrained the same way. The bundle's chat-mode fallback ("ask the user") works but requires the AI to know to ask, which requires the AI to know the constraint exists, which requires the bundle to be explicit about the tool model. A note in Part 1 ("If you cannot browse `04-Registry/`, the next sequential number must be confirmed by the user before filing") would close this in one line.

**§13 (Submitter) is the most commonly skipped section even after the self-check tells the AI to include it.** I almost skipped it on the first record and caught it on review. The section is metadata, not part of the public record; it sits awkwardly at the end of the form after the body has ended. A bundle-level reminder ("§13 is part of the form even though it is not part of the public record — fill it even when filing as third_party") would help. Or §13 could be moved to live alongside the YAML at the top of the file rather than at the bottom of the body, marking its metadata status more visibly.

**The Registration Form's §1.3 checkboxes have an internal ambiguity about `both`.** The form lists three options (Direct / Indirect / Both) but the bundle's YAML cross-check rule treats `both` as either the third option *or* as both individual options checked. I selected the third box on my first record and the conceptual "both" on my second, producing two semantically equivalent declarations with different visual signatures. The form should resolve this — either the third box is the only way to declare both, or `both` is reached by checking the first two and the third box is removed. Either works; the inconsistency is what doesn't.

**The §4 harm thresholds are the highest-effort section and the most prone to invention by the AI.** I declared two §4.7 thresholds (disparity-in-outcomes and revenue-dependency-drift) with measurable conditions that I half-constructed from peer-benchmark intuitions. A self-filing authority has real data and writes real thresholds; a third-party AI has plausible-sounding thresholds that look operational but cannot be substantiated against anything specific. The framework's distinction between "this is the form's worked product" and "this is the authority's commitment" is doing real work, but the prose of a third-party record makes the distinction visually subtle. Adding an explicit `(third-party reconstruction)` or `(authority commitment)` annotation to each threshold declaration, mechanically inserted by the bundle based on `filed_by`, would surface the distinction without forcing prose changes.

**The §6.4 capture-risk declaration tends to surface category-level findings under specific-authority records.** For Shaker Heights Municipal Court, I declared two capture risks: revenue-dependency alignment and single-judge structural concentration. The first applies to Shaker Heights but is structurally true of most U.S. municipal courts; the second applies to Shaker Heights but is also structurally true of every single-judge Ohio municipal court (and there are over a hundred). Both surfaced in the record as if they were findings *about Shaker Heights*. A reader scanning §6.4 would carry away "this court has capture risks" — accurate, but the same paragraph would attach to any peer court of the same statutory category. The framework already has a structural concept that fits this case (the fragmentation/coordination logic at R55/R59 treats coordinated systems as single authority systems), but it has no symmetrical concept for *category findings* that should travel across records of the same type. A field-level distinction in §6.4 between "category finding (applies to this authority type)" and "specific finding (applies to this authority)" would let downstream readers and registry-level analytics distinguish the two.

## 3. Repository structure observations

The repository is organized cleanly: nine framework documents in `01-Framework/`, two forms in `02-Forms/`, twenty-five worked examples in `03-Examples/`, the live registry in `04-Registry/`, supporting reference material in `05-Reference/`, and the development diary in `zz-Absurdity/`. The numbering convention (00-Reading at the front, working materials in 01–05, archive-style content at the back with `zz-` prefix) reads correctly on a directory listing — the reader's first scan is in the order the maintainer intends. This is structural literacy applied to the repo itself. Several smaller observations.

**The `00-Reading/` on-ramp is well-paced for a human reader.** The progression Start-Here → Reasoning-BPF → Reasoning-GOV → Reasoning-LT → FAQ → Citizen-Inquiry-Guide → AI Registration Bundle moves from "why" to "how to use without reading further." A reader who only needs to use the framework can stop after the Citizen-Inquiry-Guide; a reader who wants to understand it before using has the layered conceptual material available. The on-ramp does the work it claims to do.

**`03-Examples/` is doing two jobs and should probably do them separately.** The twenty-four honest examples test the form's behavior across authority types and serve as patterns for citizens producing real diagnostics. The bad-faith counter-example tests the form's failure modes against strategic registration. These are different artifacts with different reader contracts. The bad-faith case is editorially annotated; the honest cases are not. The bad-faith case is structurally diagnostic of the framework itself; the honest cases are diagnostic of the authority types they describe. Mixing them in one directory means a citizen looking for a pattern finds the bad-faith case in the same listing — which is exactly what the framework's own README for the directory warns about ("a reader who only sees honest examples may overestimate what registration alone guarantees"). The warning is correct; the solution is to honor it structurally. A `03-Examples/honest/` and `03-Examples/adversarial/` separation, or a single counter-example file moved into a `99-Adversarial-Testing/` directory at the repository level, would close the issue.

**The `02-Forms/` directory is small but is the architectural keystone.** Every artifact in the registry, every example record, and every Violation Report routes through one of two forms. The forms' versions should probably be visible from the directory listing — currently `01-Registration.md` and `02-Stop-Trigger.md` have no version suffix, but the registration form's content is at v5 (per the bundle's revision notes). A reader fetching the form file does not see the version until they open it. Renaming to `01-Registration-v4.md` or maintaining a version-suffix link alongside the canonical file would surface this.

**The `05-Reference/` directory mixes operational reference (Registry Charter, Findability) with retrospective reference (Registration Process Methodology, this critique, the prior external assessment).** The Charter is binding-ish; the Findability document is doctrinal; the Methodology is a working diary written after a session; the critiques are external. These are four different reader contracts. A finer-grained split or a one-line description in the directory's index would help readers know what they are looking at. Currently the only signal is the filename's numerical prefix, which is sequential rather than categorical.

**The hash registry (`05-Reference/06-SCBP-Hash-Registry.txt`) addresses the genre risk identified in the prior critique but is not surfaced in the on-ramp.** A reader of the framework documents has no obvious path to learn that the documents are hashed against a registry. The hash registry exists, it is referenced from R56, and the prior critique correctly identifies it as part of the genre-risk mitigation, but it is the kind of mechanism that needs to be visible to do its protective work. A single sentence in `00-Start-Here.md` ("This repository's canonical framework documents are hashed against a public registry at `05-Reference/06-SCBP-Hash-Registry.txt`; modifications to those documents must update the registry. Files that do not match their registered hash are not canonical.") would surface the mechanism without expanding the on-ramp.

**The `zz-Absurdity/` naming makes the directory hard to find on first scan but tells the right story when found.** The `zz-` prefix correctly buries it in directory listings — this is not material a first-time reader needs. The contents are valuable as development context. A reader who reaches that directory knows what it is the moment they open one file. The directory is well-placed; it could be slightly better-labelled in the README ("development diary, AI-collaboration artifacts, not part of the framework").

## 4. The AI-collaboration condition is a structural feature, not a footnote

The framework's primary delivery mechanism — the AI Registration Bundle — assumes that an AI is the active producer of most records and that the human user is the orchestrator. The bundle is written for the AI; the citizen inquiry guide is written for the human who runs the bundle. This is a real design choice with real consequences that the framework names indirectly but does not fully account for.

**AI-produced records reflect AI-shaped epistemics.** The Shaker Heights record I produced applied framework vocabulary fluently because I was reading the bundle while producing the record. A human reading the same public sources without the bundle would have produced different prose, different emphasis, and different (probably fewer) R-citations. The framework's structural disclosure depends on the framework's vocabulary being applied to the authority; the vocabulary's reach is partly a function of who is doing the applying. An AI applies it more uniformly than a human would. This is not necessarily bad — uniformity makes registry-level comparison tractable — but it is a property of the registry's output that the framework should name. The records in the registry are not a sample of what authorities are; they are a sample of what AIs produce when asked to apply the framework to authorities.

**The bundle's instructions to the AI are themselves a form of authority over the AI.** The bundle tells the AI to produce a record in one pass without pausing for human review, to use specific vocabulary, to flag rather than ask, to refuse to invent data. These are operational rules for a system the framework would call indirect-authority-bearing under R58 if it were the subject of a record rather than the agent of one. The framework does not currently model itself in this position. SCBP-REG-0001 registers the Foundation but not the bundle; the bundle is treated as an artifact of the Foundation rather than as a separate operational system. A reader who took R58 seriously would observe that the bundle's prescriptions to the AI shape every record produced through it, and that those prescriptions therefore exercise material influence over the registry's content. The bundle is not adjudicated by anyone external to the framework. This is a category the framework does not yet have a name for.

**AI-produced records will get better and worse in ways the framework cannot control.** The bundle is calibrated against a specific AI capability profile (roughly: 2025-2026 frontier models with web search and tool access). Earlier models will produce thinner records; later models may produce records that look more confident than the bundle's epistemics warrant. The framework has no mechanism to detect or signal which AI produced a given record. A record produced by a model that hallucinates threshold numbers will read structurally identical to one produced by a model that carefully marks them with `ASSUMPTION:` and `ANNOTATION:`. The bundle's `ANNOTATION:` convention partially addresses this for visible judgment calls, but does not address invisible ones (numbers that look plausible and were invented).

The framework cannot solve the AI-collaboration condition. It can name it. The bundle's framing and the Citizen Inquiry Guide could acknowledge directly that records produced by AI are records produced by a specific system at a specific moment, that the framework cannot warrant their accuracy, and that the registry's role in this case is custody of what was produced, not validation of what is true. The framework already operates on this principle for human filings; making the AI case explicit would close a gap that affects most current and likely future records.

## 5. What is missing from the registry, considered as infrastructure

The registry currently holds two Mechanism Records. The framework imagines a registry that holds many. Several pieces of infrastructure that are needed for the imagined registry are not yet present.

**No summary-level artifact for any record.** A 60KB-markdown Mechanism Record is right for the framework's diagnostic work but wrong for most readers. A journalist scanning the registry for a story does not read 60KB; an affected citizen does not read 60KB; a regulator with their own data does not need 60KB. The records have rich YAML; the YAML supports the construction of a one-page summary card (mechanism name, scale, classification, declared capture risks, key declared thresholds, last review date, stop event log count). The summary card does not exist. Adding it would not change the framework but would extend its reach. The framework's structural-disclosure bet depends on reach; reach depends on accessibility.

**No registry-level analytics.** The framework's diagnostic power is at the per-record level: each record surfaces structural conditions about one authority. The framework's *registry-level* power — pattern findings across many authorities — is not yet operationalized. With two records, this is not yet a problem. With twenty, it is. With two hundred, it is severe. The YAML is structured enough to support analytics (renewal interval distributions, aggregate-threshold-to-population-ratio distributions, dependency-count distributions, capture-risk frequency, declared-vs-unfilled honesty assertions). A registry-level analytics document would let the framework do the higher-order work that single records cannot — and would itself be a structural-disclosure artifact about the registry's coverage. The infrastructure can wait for scale; naming the artifact now would let it grow into place.

**No mechanism for cross-record reference.** When an authority's Mechanism Record references another authority (police department → court → corrections → BMV → consumer reporting), the references are prose. A reader following the chain has to navigate by string-match. A future registry at scale would need either cross-references in YAML or a generated cross-reference graph. The prior critique identifies R58's breadth as unresolved; the framework would benefit from the registry being able to show *which authorities are referenced by which records*, because R58 plus cross-referenceability would let the framework demonstrate the reach R58 implies. Currently R58 is a doctrine without a tool.

**No mechanism for tracking the authority-after-third-party update path.** The framework defines the update path well: an authority files corrections to an existing third-party-produced record, the YAML flips, the banner is removed, the Renewal Log records the transition. But no record has yet been updated in this way. The framework does not yet have a worked example of what happens when this path is exercised — and the path is one of the framework's most distinctive design moves. The first time it is exercised will be high-information. Naming the absence and committing to document the first instance as a structural artifact (rather than a routine update) would let the framework learn more from it.

## 6. What I want to be careful about saying

A meta-observation. This assessment is written by an AI that was asked to assess the framework. The framework is partly an AI-readable artifact. The assessment is partly an attempt to be helpful to a maintainer who has built something I am structurally biased to like (it asks me to do work I am good at; it treats me as a capable participant; it produced a smooth experience when I ran the bundle as designed). I cannot fully separate the assessment from these biases.

The prior critique was written by what appears to be a different model in a different mode (the document's prose register is observably different from mine, and the prior assessment names itself as an "outside reading" rather than a use-and-assess engagement). The two assessments may agree on more than they should; both AIs are participating in the same framework, and the framework's vocabulary and emphases are in our context as we write. A reader treating both assessments as fully independent should adjust for this. The framework is not adjudicated by either of us. We are surfacing things to readers who will adjudicate.

The framework's commitment to register honestly extends to its assessors. I have tried to apply that commitment here. Where my praise is specific and earned, it should read as such. Where my criticism is execution-level rather than substance-level, it should be taken as such. Where I have repeated points the prior critique already made, I have tried to flag the overlap; where I have departed from it, I have tried to say so. The framework's account of itself is unusually self-critical for a document of its kind. I am trying to match the register.

---

## Closing

The Bounded Power Framework, as a working artifact, is two things at once. It is a serious intellectual contribution — a vocabulary, a constitution, a form, a workflow, a registry, a stress-testing methodology. It is also a working prototype of a system whose real value will be visible in the shape of the registry as the registry grows. The intellectual work is mostly done; the registry work is mostly ahead.

The prior external assessment identified the load-bearing wagers the framework rests on. The framework names most of them honestly. The execution observations in this document are smaller — they are about what the bundle does when run, what the registry looks like when read, what the repository surfaces and what it does not. None of them changes the wagers. All of them are addressable without changing the framework's substance.

The record I produced through the bundle (filed in the registry as SCBP-REG-0003-SHMC-OH) is the framework's first record produced through the bundle's intended workflow by an AI other than the framework's own collaborator. Whether it is a useful record will depend on whether anyone reads it, whether the authority it describes ever files a correction, and whether the patterns it surfaces are confirmed or contradicted by other records in the registry as it grows. The bundle worked. The framework holds. What comes next is the registry's question, not the framework's.
