**2.3 — Non-consenting portion:**
The framework's "voluntary-but-stuck" test (§2.3) applies in a distinctive way for healthcare:
- *Emergency department patients* are non-consenting in a meaningful sense: an unconscious trauma patient, a person in active suicidal crisis, a pregnant person in active labor, or any person with a true medical emergency cannot meaningfully choose a different hospital at the moment of need. EMTALA recognizes this structurally by mandating screening and stabilization regardless of ability to pay.
- *Patients subject to pink-slip holds* under ORC §5122 are non-consenting by definition — they are being held against their stated will because they are judged a danger to self or others.
- *Incarcerated patients, court-ordered patients, minor children, and patients lacking decision-making capacity* are non-consenting.
- *Patients in narrow-network insurance plans* may face out-of-network costs or denial of coverage at competing hospitals, effectively channeling them to UH.
- *Geographic concentration:* in some service-area communities, UH is the only or principal hospital. Cuyahoga County has two major systems (UH and Cleveland Clinic); other counties may have only one major option.
- *Minor children residing with patient parents* are subject to UH determinations (e.g., mandatory child-protection reporting based on parent conduct; genetic information implications) without consenting.
- *Embryos and reproductive materials* — the 2018 UH Fertility Clinic incident, in which 4,000+ stored eggs and embryos were destroyed when freezer alarms had been deactivated, affected approximately 950 families. The legal status of embryos and the appropriate damages framework were contested in subsequent litigation. The framework treats the affected families as non-consenting third parties for the loss event (they consented to storage, not to negligent loss).
- *Patients consenting to scheduled care* are technically consenting but operate under information asymmetry, complex consent documents, and time pressure — substantively non-consenting in framework terms in many situations.

For SCBP-09 §II purposes, the non-consenting population is approximately 200,000, reflecting:
- Emergency department patient population annually
- Inpatient admissions annually
- Pediatric patient population
- Behavioral health and pink-slip-hold patient population
- Employees in coercive economic relationships per §2.4 below

This places the authority in the SCBP-09 §II 100,000–1,000,000 band, with threshold range 10–50 in 90 days.

ANNOTATION: this is the largest non-consenting population estimate in any record produced in this registry, reflecting both the broad scope of UH operations and the framework's structural recognition that medical authority over patients is substantively non-consenting in many circumstances even when paperwork suggests consent.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Federal Regulatory and Reimbursement Authorities (CMS, OIG, FDA, OCR)**
- Who can activate it: Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) through Conditions of Participation enforcement, denial of payment, or termination of provider agreement; HHS Office of Inspector General (OIG) audits; HHS Office for Civil Rights (Section 1557, HIPAA enforcement); FDA enforcement (for clinical research and tissue regulations); Drug Enforcement Administration (controlled substances); CDC and other federal authorities through reportable conditions
- Trigger condition: threshold-based — federal statutory and regulatory standards
- What "stopped" means: termination of Medicare/Medicaid provider agreement (effectively closes the hospital — Medicare/Medicaid revenue is generally a majority of hospital revenue); Conditions of Participation enforcement; civil monetary penalties; consent decrees; FDA shutdown of specific clinical research; criminal referral
- Independence justification: federal agencies operate outside UH structure
- Post-stop review process and timeline: variable; CoP enforcement can be rapid for immediate-jeopardy findings; OIG audits typically multi-year processes

ANNOTATION: this is the strongest stop-path category for healthcare authorities. CMS termination is functionally an existential threat to a large hospital system due to revenue concentration.

**STOP PATH 2 — Joint Commission (or Equivalent) Accreditation Body**
- Who can activate it: The Joint Commission (TJC), DNV GL Healthcare, or other CMS-deemed accreditation body
- Trigger condition: accreditation-standard threshold breach; immediate-jeopardy findings during surveys
- What "stopped" means: preliminary denial of accreditation; conditional accreditation; denial of accreditation (which jeopardizes deemed status for Medicare/Medicaid participation)
- Independence justification: TJC and equivalent bodies are independent accreditation organizations operating outside individual hospital systems
- Post-stop review process and timeline: TJC survey cycle approximately every three years; immediate-jeopardy findings trigger rapid response cycles

**STOP PATH 3 — Tort Litigation, State Regulatory Action, and Patient/Community Advocacy**
