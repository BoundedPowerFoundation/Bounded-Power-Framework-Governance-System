## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Shaker Heights City Planning Commission (CPC).

**1.2 — Functional description:**
The City Planning Commission is a five-member appointed body of the City of Shaker Heights that exercises decision and recommendation authority over land use within the corporate limits of the City. Its decisions and recommendations attach to applications brought under the Zoning Ordinance (Codified Ordinances Part Twelve) and to City actions affecting public land. Specifically, the Commission:
- **Decides** applications for Conditional Use Permits.
- **Decides** site plans submitted for review under §1213.06.
- **Decides** applications for re-subdivision of land.
- **Recommends** to City Council amendments to the Zoning Code text and the Zoning Map (Council retains final decision authority by ordinance).
- **Recommends** to City Council improvements to public land under §1212.02(I), with administrative approval by Council.
- Operates jointly with the Board of Zoning Appeals (BZA), which shares the same membership but exercises a separate variance-granting authority under §1212.05.

A favorable decision is a precondition to the issuance by the Building Commissioner of a Certificate of Occupancy, building permit, or land-use permit for any matter requiring CPC review. The Commission's decisions are appealable to the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 2506.

**1.3 — Authority classification:**
[ ] Direct
[ ] Indirect
[x] Both — describe:
The Commission exercises **direct** authority over Conditional Use Permits, site plans, and re-subdivision applications: its approval or denial is itself the operative decision. The Commission exercises **indirect** authority through its recommendations to Council on zoning text and map amendments and on improvements to public land: those recommendations materially shape Council's legislative action without binding it. Under R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition), the recommending function is itself authority for compliance purposes.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 — Geographic reach:**
[x] Single municipality — the corporate limits of the City of Shaker Heights, Ohio. Per §1210.04(A), the Zoning Ordinance applies to all land, uses, buildings, and structures within those limits. The Commission has no authority outside the City.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest action CPC can take against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Deny a Conditional Use Permit** — preventing a property owner from establishing the proposed use at the proposed location. Effect: the use cannot lawfully begin without successful appeal or successful amendment of the Zoning Ordinance.
- **Approve a Conditional Use Permit with conditions** — binding the permittee to operating conditions (hours of operation, parking minimums, screening, traffic mitigation, occupancy caps) enforceable through the Zoning Administrator's permit-revocation authority under §1212.06.
- **Deny site plan approval** — preventing construction or alteration from proceeding to building permit until the deficiency is cured.
- **Recommend denial of a zoning amendment** to Council. Council retains final authority but a CPC negative recommendation is procedurally weighted.
- **Deny re-subdivision** — preventing the lot reconfiguration the applicant sought.

The Commission does not assess civil penalties on its own initiative, does not order abatement (that is the Zoning Administrator's and Building Commissioner's function under §1212.06 and Chapter 1214), does not condemn property, and does not collect fines. Enforcement of Commission decisions runs through the Zoning Administrator and, if violations persist, through prosecution under Chapter 1214 in the Shaker Heights Municipal Court.

The maximum monetary impact on an applicant from a Commission denial is the cost of the foregone use plus the cost of subsequent appeal under R.C. Chapter 2506. The maximum monetary impact on a neighbor from a Commission approval is the property-value, quality-of-life, and use-and-enjoyment effect of the approved use — which is not bounded by the Commission's authority but by the substance of the use itself.

**3.3 — Resource ceiling:**
- **Maximum operating budget:** Not separately appropriated. The Commission is supported by the City of Shaker Heights Planning Department under Codified Ordinances Chapter 131, with staff costs and meeting costs absorbed in the Planning Department line item of the City's General Fund. UNKNOWN specific dollar amount.
- **Maximum staff (FTE):** Zero direct staff; Commission members are appointed volunteers, with one ex officio Council member who is otherwise compensated for Council service. Staff support is provided by the Planning Department (Planning Director, planners, Zoning Administrator). ANNOTATION: A reviewer with access to the City's classification plan can populate the Planning Department FTE count.
- **Maximum data systems and data volume:** Permit-portal application system; agenda and minutes published through the City's website; staff reports prepared by the Planning Department.

**3.4 — Explicit exclusions — what this authority may NOT do:**
[x] Geographic exclusions: The Commission may not exercise any authority outside the corporate limits of Shaker Heights.
[x] Action exclusions: The Commission may not amend the Zoning Code or Zoning Map by its own action; it may only recommend such amendments to Council, which decides by ordinance. The Commission may not grant variances; that authority lies with the Board of Zoning Appeals under §1212.05 (though the BZA shares membership with the CPC, the two bodies exercise distinct statutory authorities). The Commission may not enforce the Zoning Ordinance through citation, arrest, or civil action; enforcement is the Zoning Administrator's function under §1212.06 and Chapter 1214. The Commission may not condemn property under eminent domain.
[x] Data exclusions: The Commission may not retain applicant-submitted financial, business-confidential, or proprietary information beyond the periods required by Ohio municipal records retention schedules; applicants may request confidential treatment for trade-secret material under applicable law. The Commission may not disclose Social Security numbers, financial account numbers, or other personal identifiers exempt under O.R.C. §149.43.
[x] Procedural exclusions: The Commission may not act on a matter without a duly noticed public hearing where notice is required by Part Twelve or by R.C. Chapter 713. The Commission may not act in violation of the Open Meetings Act (R.C. §121.22), which restricts deliberation in executive session.
[x] Temporal exclusions: The Commission may not act on a matter at a meeting where it lacks a quorum (three of five members).
[x] Other exclusions: The Commission may not adjudicate matters in which a member has a personal financial interest under Ohio Ethics Law (R.C. Chapter 102); a conflicted member must recuse. The Commission may not consider matters reserved exclusively to the Landmark Commission (proposed changes to Landmarks and structures in Landmark Districts under §1212.04) without coordination with that body.

**3.5 — Indirect influence boundaries:**
The Commission's indirect-authority outputs (per §1.3) are:
- **Recommendations to Council on Zoning Code text amendments.** Used by: City Council, in its legislative action. Decision shaped: the content and adoption of zoning ordinance amendments.
- **Recommendations to Council on Zoning Map amendments.** Used by: City Council. Decision shaped: rezoning of specific parcels.
- **Recommendations to Council on improvements to public land under §1212.02(I).** Used by: City Council. Decision shaped: administrative approval of public-land projects (e.g., Doan Brook Restoration at Horseshoe Park, Hildana Park improvements as appearing in 2025 Council agendas).
- **Staff-report posture.** The Commission's questions, concerns, and articulated standards in any one proceeding shape how the Planning Department drafts subsequent staff reports and how applicants structure subsequent applications.

**3.6 — Anti-proxy declaration:**
The framework concern most relevant to the Commission is whether Conditional Use Permit conditions, site-plan conditions, or re-subdivision conditions are used to achieve effects that the Zoning Ordinance itself does not authorize the Commission to impose directly. ANNOTATION: Public sources do not show an explicit anti-proxy commitment of this kind. Practical anti-proxy concerns include: imposing affordability, demographic, or speech-related conditions in the guise of operational conditions; imposing conditions on tenants or customers (rather than the applicant) that the Commission could not impose by zoning amendment; using condition-stacking to render an approved use uneconomic, achieving a de facto denial without the appealable record a denial would produce.

*Rules addressed: R04 (Functional Effect), R35 (Scope Narrowing Default), R55 (Fragmentation), R66 (Anti-Proxy), R74 (Proxy Equivalence Expansion). SCBP-09 §VII (Geographic Specificity), §VIII (Coercive Ceiling Specificity).*

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**
[x] Not applicable — justification: The Commission's authorized actions are land-use decisions and recommendations. None of them involves direct physical contact with persons or direct physical action upon persons. Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, more than 10% of Commission decisions are reversed or remanded on appeal under R.C. ## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Judicial appeal under R.C. Chapter 2506.**
- Who can activate it: Any party adversely affected by a final decision of the Commission — applicant, neighbor, or affected party with standing — by filing a notice of appeal in the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas within 30 days of the decision.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on the existence of a final administrative decision. The court reviews the decision under the §2506.04 standard (whether the decision is unconstitutional, illegal, arbitrary, capricious, unreasonable, or unsupported by the preponderance of substantial, reliable, and probative evidence).
- What "stopped" means: A specific Commission decision is reversed, modified, or remanded. In persistent-pattern cases, a structural injunction is theoretically available but rare.
- Independence justification: The Court of Common Pleas is constitutionally separate from the City of Shaker Heights municipal executive and legislative branches. Judges are elected county-wide, not appointed by City officials, and are not financially dependent on the Commission or the City.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ordinary appellate process to the Ohio Eighth District Court of Appeals and (discretionarily) to the Ohio Supreme Court.

**STOP PATH 2 — City Council legislative override and budgetary action.**
- Who can activate it: The Shaker Heights City Council, by ordinance.
- Trigger condition: Both threshold-based (Council's own legislative judgment on zoning amendments — Council can reject a CPC recommendation, or amend the Zoning Code to remove a class of decisions from the Commission's scope) and judgment-based (a Council member's introduction of a relevant ordinance).
- What "stopped" means: A class of matters is removed from CPC jurisdiction; a recommended amendment is rejected; the Zoning Ordinance is restructured to alter the Commission's authority; in the limit case, the Commission's enabling sections of Chapter 1212 are amended or repealed (subject to Charter Article V, Section 3, which establishes the Commission and may itself be amended only by voter referendum).
- Independence justification: Council is elected by Shaker Heights voters. Commissioners are appointed by the Mayor with Council confirmation; Council does not depend on Commission decisions for its own authority or revenue. The Mayor is a member of the Commission (and historically its Chair), creating a partial overlap with the executive — a structural feature noted in §6.4.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Council action is itself the public record; Charter-permitted referendum is available if a citizen petition meets signature requirements.

**STOP PATH 3 — Voter referendum / Charter amendment.**
- Who can activate it: The voters of Shaker Heights, by initiative or referendum petition meeting the signature thresholds in the Charter and in R.C. Chapter 731. Charter Article V, Section 3 may itself be amended by voter approval.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on signature collection and ballot certification.
- What "stopped" means: Charter amendment can dissolve the Commission, restructure its membership, alter its powers, or transfer its authority to a different body. Ordinance referendum can override Council enactments related to the Zoning Ordinance.
- Independence justification: The voters are not part of the Commission's institutional structure. The referendum process operates without the Commission's cooperation.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ballot certification and election timeline under Ohio election law.

**5.4 — Penalty for activating stop:**
[x] Confirmed — no penalty applies. A party filing an R.C. Successful appellants may recover certain costs; unsuccessful appellants do not face Commission-imposed sanctions.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 — Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):**

*Dependency A — City of Shaker Heights Planning Department.* Function: drafting staff reports, conducting site visits, providing technical analysis, drafting findings of fact for Commission action, maintaining the application and records system, publishing agendas and minutes. The Planning Department's staff-report posture materially shapes Commission deliberation. The Department is a City department under Codified Ordinances Chapter 131; staff are City employees compensated on the City's classification plan. Compensation structure: flat municipal salary (not tied to permit volume or applicant outcome). ANNOTATION: A municipal-salary structure is structurally lower-risk than a permit-fee-tied structure would be; the framework concern is whether Planning Department workload pressure creates implicit prioritization of higher-fee applications.

*Dependency B — Zoning Administrator.* Function: front-line zoning compliance review, intake of applications, written interpretations of specific provisions of the Zoning Ordinance under §1213.11, staff assistance to the Commission and the BZA per §1212.06.B.2. The Zoning Administrator's interpretive decisions operationalize as the Commission's effective scope (the Administrator decides what reaches the Commission and how). Compensation structure: flat municipal salary.

**6.2 — Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with a structural relationship):**

- **City Council.** Upstream and lateral — Council adopts the Zoning Ordinance, confirms Commission appointments, decides zoning amendments on Commission recommendation, and appropriates the Planning Department budget.
- **Mayor.** Lateral and internal — the Mayor appoints Commission members and serves as Chair under §1212.03.B. The Mayor is simultaneously the chief executive of the City and the chair of the Commission, an overlap that is a structural feature of the Charter (§6.4).
- **Board of Zoning Appeals.** Lateral — shares membership with the CPC under §1212.03 but exercises a distinct variance authority under §1212.05. Decisions of one body affect the practical scope of the other (a granted variance may render a Conditional Use Permit unnecessary; a denied variance may force an application back to Council for amendment).
- **Landmark Commission.** Lateral — exercises distinct authority over properties in Landmark Districts under §1212.04. Coordination on overlapping matters is required by ordinance.
- **Architectural Review Board.** Lateral — exercises distinct authority over exterior design and materials. The City Planning Commission Working Group is a subgroup of these four bodies (CPC/BZA, ARB, Landmark Commission).
- **Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas.** Downstream — receives R.C. Chapter 2506 appeals from Commission decisions.
- **Ohio Attorney General.** Downstream — enforces the Open Meetings Act and the Public Records Act; may issue advisory opinions on R.C. Chapter 713 and Ohio Ethics Law (R.C. Chapter 102).

**6.3 — Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure whose failure would compromise stop paths or auditability):**

- **Open Meetings Act compliance infrastructure** — agenda publication, public-comment availability, minute-keeping. If these fail, R.C. Chapter 2506 appeals based on procedural error become harder to support and adversarial-exposure review (R81) is compromised.
- **Permit-portal application system.** If lost or compromised, the application record on which Commission decisions are based becomes unreliable, complicating judicial review.
- **Video archive of meetings** (Granicus / Swagit or successor). If lost, the contemporaneous record of deliberation is lost, compromising Stop Path 1.

**6.4 — Capture risk identification:**
[ ] No
[x] Yes — identify and describe the structural risk:

Three structural risks are visible from public sources:

**(a) Mayor-as-chair concentration.** Under §1212.03.B, the Mayor appoints the Commission and chairs it. The Mayor is simultaneously the City's chief executive (Charter §IV-3) and the appointing authority for the body that decides matters in which the City may be the applicant or have a public-policy stake (e.g., improvements to public land under §1212.02(I)). This is a feature of the Charter, not a deviation from it, but it concentrates appointment, agenda-setting, and chair authority in a single office. R03 (Institutional Capture Prohibition) attaches to this concentration as a structural condition to monitor.

**(b) Developer–applicant repeat-player concentration.** Land-use decisions concentrate among a small population of repeat-player applicants (regional developers, institutional property owners). Repeat-player applicants accumulate experience with Commission preferences and Planning Department processes that one-time applicants and neighbors do not have. R50 (Capture Risk Monitoring) attaches to this asymmetry.

**(c) Specialized professional dominance.** The Commission and its staff support concentrate planning, architecture, and real-estate-law expertise that neighbors and tenants typically lack. Procedural rights formally equal between applicants and objectors are practically unequal due to expertise asymmetry. R69 (Burden of Proof Reversal) attaches structurally.

ANNOTATION: None of these is a deviation from how comparable municipal planning commissions operate across Ohio and the United States. Their identification is structural disclosure under R39, not a finding of misconduct.

**6.5 — Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**
The Commission's decisions, once made and acted upon, are highly persistent: a Conditional Use Permit, once issued and operationalized, generally persists with the use; a site-plan-approved building, once constructed, persists for its physical life; a zoning amendment, once enacted by Council on Commission recommendation, persists until repealed. The principal reversibility-affecting dependency is the durability of the built environment — once a structure is built under a Commission-approved site plan, reversing the decision does not unbuild the structure.

**6.6 — Coordination disclosures:**
The Commission coordinates with the BZA (shared membership), Landmark Commission, Architectural Review Board, and Planning Department through the City Planning Commission Working Group and through cross-referenced ordinance provisions. This coordination does not function as a single shared authority system in the R55/R59 sense: each body retains its own statutory authority and acts on its own record. R55 (Fragmentation) concerns would arise only if the Working Group began to substitute for individual-body deliberation in a way that obscured which body made which decision.

**6.8 — External authority operationalization (R72):**
- **Planning Department staff-report content.** When the Department's staff report recommends approval with conditions and the Commission adopts the recommendation without modification, the affected party experiences the Commission's authority but the underlying decision is substantially the Department's. R72 treats the staff-report-followed pattern as operationalized external authority.
- **Mayor's agenda-setting and chairing.** The order in which matters are heard and the framing of Commission discussion is set by the Chair (the Mayor or designee). The affected party experiences a Commission decision but the procedural framing was the Chair's. R72 attaches.

The affected party can usually distinguish a Commission decision from a staff recommendation by reading the staff report and the meeting minutes; this is a low but non-zero R72 burden.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**
ASSUMPTION: 24 months (approximately 720 days) for full wind-down. Justification: A Charter-established body whose existence is fixed by Article V, Section 3 cannot be dissolved by ordinance alone; dissolution requires (a) Charter amendment by voter referendum, (b) transition of pending matters to a successor decision-maker, and (c) restructuring of the Zoning Ordinance to remove cross-references to the Commission. The 24-month estimate accommodates ballot-certification timing, an election cycle, transition planning, and ordinance restructuring.

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**
- **Months 1–6:** Council action authorizing a Charter amendment to dissolve or restructure the Commission; ballot certification; voter campaign period; election. (If only operational changes are sought rather than dissolution, Council action by ordinance is the principal step and the timeline shortens to 6–9 months.)
- **Months 6–12:** Transition of pending matters to the successor decision-maker (Council acting in plenary session under its R.C. Chapter 713 authority; or a restructured body); ordinance restructuring; staff reassignment within the Planning Department.
- **Months 12–18:** Closeout of long-pending applications under the prior framework; archive of records under municipal retention schedules; final adjudication of any R.C. Chapter 2506 appeals filed against pre-dissolution decisions.
- **Months 18–24:** Final disposition of records and standing orders; closeout of any related litigation.

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- **Charter establishment.** The Commission exists under Charter Article V, Section 3; dissolution requires voter approval.
- **Pending applications.** Matters under active deliberation at the moment of dissolution must be transitioned to a successor decision-maker with notice to applicants.
- **Cross-referenced ordinance structure.** Part Twelve of the Codified Ordinances references the Commission throughout; restructuring is non-trivial.
- **Conditional Use Permits in effect.** Outstanding CUPs were issued by the Commission; transition of monitoring and enforcement authority is required.
- **Body interlock.** The Commission shares membership with the Board of Zoning Appeals under §1212.03; dissolving one without the other requires careful structural design.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**
- **Personal data (applicant contact information, neighbor objections with addresses):** Retained per Ohio municipal records retention schedule; transferred to successor under municipal records continuity.
- **Institutional records (applications, staff reports, decisions, minutes, video):** Retained as permanent or long-retention records under the municipal schedule; archived to City clerk; remains accessible under R.C. §149.43.
- **Derived outputs (Conditional Use Permits, site-plan approvals, recommended amendments):** Remain in force unless and until rescinded by the successor authority or by Council; R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) attaches to each outstanding decision.
- **Audit logs (meeting recordings, agenda systems):** Retained per Granicus/Swagit retention agreement and municipal schedule.

**9.5 — Downstream effect reversibility:**
Outputs propagated to other systems include: building permits issued in reliance on site-plan approval (Building Commissioner records); occupancy certificates issued in reliance on CUP approval (Building Commissioner records); zoning-map amendments enacted by Council on Commission recommendation (permanent ordinance record); county property records reflecting re-subdivision approvals. On termination of the Commission, none of these is automatically invalidated; reversal of any one of them requires action by the successor authority, by Council, or by the affected property owner.

**9.6 — Replacement feasibility:**
The Commission's functions could be transferred to (a) Council acting in plenary session (under R.C. Chapter 713 such transfer is permissible), (b) a restructured combined land-use board absorbing the CPC, BZA, ARB, and Landmark Commission functions, or (c) an administrative-hearing officer for routine decisions with appeal to a Council committee. Replacement is feasible. The structural choice is whether to consolidate or fragment land-use decision-making.

**9.7 — Reversibility verification:**
The declared 24-month timeline is estimated, not verified. ANNOTATION: The City of Shaker Heights has not, in publicly available materials, rehearsed dissolution of the Commission. Per R43, the §9.1 declaration is treated as estimated.
