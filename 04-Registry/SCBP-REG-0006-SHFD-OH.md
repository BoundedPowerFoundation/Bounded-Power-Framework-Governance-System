## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Shaker Heights Fire Department.

**1.2 — Functional description:**
The Shaker Heights Fire Department (SHFD) is a career municipal fire and emergency medical services agency operating within the corporate limits of the City of Shaker Heights, Ohio. It performs fire suppression, fire prevention inspection and code enforcement under the Ohio Fire Code as locally adopted, plain-language fire investigation, hazardous-materials response, technical rescue, and pre-hospital emergency medical services including basic and advanced life support and patient transport to area hospitals. It may, under the Ohio Fire Code and local Codified Ordinances Part Nine (Fire Prevention Code), enter premises for inspection, order abatement of fire hazards, condemn occupancies as unsafe, issue notices of violation, and require remedial action. During an active fire or emergency incident, the senior officer on scene may direct evacuation, restrict access, commandeer reasonable assistance, and take measures to control the incident. The department also issues fire-prevention permits for regulated activities (open burning, tents, flammable storage, hot work). It bills affected parties and their health insurers for EMS transport service.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 — Geographic reach:**
[x] Single municipality — corporate limits of the City of Shaker Heights, Ohio (approximately 6.3 square miles). Specialty-team response (Hillcrest Technical Rescue Team, Chagrin/Southeast Regional Hazardous Materials Response Team, Hillcrest-Heights Regional Fire Investigation Unit) extends operationally into eastern Cuyahoga County and into Geauga County for HAZMAT.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest action SHFD can take against a person, organization, or premises:
- **On-scene incident command:** direct evacuation of a structure; restrict access to a fire or emergency scene; commandeer reasonable assistance from bystanders; force entry to a structure where fire or life-safety conditions are believed to exist (exigent circumstances doctrine).
- **Fire code enforcement:** issue notice of violation; order abatement of identified hazards within a stated cure period; condemn an occupancy as unsafe and order it vacated; deny or revoke a fire-prevention permit; refer for prosecution as a minor misdemeanor or other classification provided by the Ohio Fire Code and Shaker Heights Codified Ordinances Part Nine.
- **EMS:** detain a patient for assessment under capacity-incapacity doctrine; transport without express consent under implied-consent doctrine when patient is incapacitated; restrain a patient when necessary for clinical safety and pursuant to Ohio EMS protocol.
- **Mandatory reporting:** suspected child abuse/neglect, suspected elder abuse, suspected arson, deaths required to be reported to the Cuyahoga County Medical Examiner.

The Department does not possess law-enforcement arrest authority; it does not write civil monetary penalties on its own initiative outside of the fee schedule for permits and EMS transport.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — City Council resolution / budget action.**
- Who can activate it: The Shaker Heights City Council, by majority vote, may suspend or restructure Department operations through ordinance, charter-permitted action, or budget resolution that withdraws appropriation.
- Trigger condition: Both threshold-based (e.g., budget cycle) and judgment-based (a Council member's introduction of a resolution).
- What "stopped" means: Depending on the resolution: suspension of a specific Departmental practice, reduction or elimination of staffing, withdrawal of fire-prevention authority, or in the limit case, dissolution of the Department as a municipal function and reorganization under contract or regional arrangement.
- Independence justification: The Council is elected by Shaker Heights voters. Department members do not sit on Council. Council does not depend on Department-generated revenue for its own funding base. The Council–Department relationship is hierarchical, with the Council and Mayor superior.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Council action is itself the public record; further review occurs through the regular legislative process and through Charter-permitted referendum if a citizen petition meets signature requirements.

**STOP PATH 2 — Judicial order (Ohio courts).**
- Who can activate it: Any person with standing — a property owner subject to an abatement order, a patient claiming injury from EMS action, a person aggrieved by a code-enforcement decision — by filing in the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas, Shaker Heights Municipal Court (for the relevant jurisdictional matters), or federal court (for federal claims).
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on a justiciable claim (constitutional, statutory, tort).
- What "stopped" means: Specific actions enjoined; specific enforcement decisions vacated; damages awarded; in the extraordinary case, structural injunction against a practice.
- Independence justification: The Ohio judiciary is constitutionally separate from the municipal executive. Judges are not appointed or supervised by Shaker Heights officials. The courts are not financially dependent on SHFD operations.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ordinary appellate process under the Ohio Rules of Appellate Procedure.

**STOP PATH 3 — State and federal regulatory override.**
- Who can activate it: The Ohio State Fire Marshal (over Ohio Fire Code application); the Ohio Department of Public Safety, Division of EMS (over EMS certification and protocol compliance under O.R.C. Chapter 4765); the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights (over HIPAA compliance); the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission and the Ohio Civil Rights Commission (over employment matters); the Cuyahoga County Medical Examiner (over death-investigation matters within the county).
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on regulatory determination of non-compliance.
- What "stopped" means: Revocation or suspension of EMS provider certification (which would prevent the Department from transporting patients); imposition of corrective action plans; civil monetary penalties for HIPAA violations; consent decrees.
- Independence justification: State and federal regulators are structurally outside the Department's chain of command and are not funded by Shaker Heights.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ohio Administrative Procedure Act for state-level matters; federal Administrative Procedure Act for federal matters.

ANNOTATION: A fourth stop path that would meaningfully strengthen structural alignment — an independent local civilian review body with authority over fire and EMS operations — does not appear to exist in Shaker Heights. The Civil Service Commission has authority over fire-service appointment and promotion criteria but not over operational practice. R32 (Independent Stop Authority) is satisfied by the three above; the absence of a fourth, locally-scaled, civilian review body is a structural observation.

**6.8 — External authority operationalization (R72):**
Two operationalized external authorities are present:

The affected party may not always be able to distinguish SHFD's decision from the external decision. R72 treats both as part of SHFD's authority for compliance purposes.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**
ASSUMPTION: 18 months (approximately 540 days) for full wind-down. Justification: A two-station, ~67-firefighter career department serving a 6.3-square-mile densely populated city cannot be dissolved on a short timeline without producing a coverage gap. The dismantling period must accommodate (a) successor-provider procurement or regional-arrangement renegotiation, (b) collective-bargaining transition for personnel under the IAFF Local 516 contract, (c) apparatus and station disposition, (d) records-retention transition for patient and incident records, and (e) ratepayer/taxpayer notice obligations.

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**
- **Months 1–3:** Council action authorizing wind-down; public notice to residents and to mutual-aid partners; successor-provider procurement (regional district, contract with neighboring department, or other arrangement); inventory of apparatus, stations, and equipment; records-management transition planning.
- **Months 3–9:** Personnel transition under collective bargaining; rolling station closure with successor coverage in place; apparatus disposition; permit and inspection backlog completion.
- **Months 9–15:** Closeout of fire-prevention permit obligations; transition of code-enforcement files to building-and-housing or successor provider; transition of EMS records to successor under HIPAA-compliant business-associate arrangement.
- **Months 15–18:** Final records retention period for outstanding billing and litigation matters; archive transfer to City clerk under Ohio retention schedules.

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- **Mutual-aid network dependency.** Neighboring departments' staffing and apparatus assume SHFD presence; absorbing SHFD's call volume requires their capacity, not just the City's decision.
- **Personnel collective-bargaining obligations.** IAFF Local 516 has had a continuous bargaining presence since 1937; transition terms are governed by the active CBA.
- **EMS provider certification.** Successor provider must hold Ohio EMS certification; this is not transferable.
- **Pension and OPEB obligations** to retirees through the Ohio Police and Fire Pension Fund are not extinguished by Department dissolution.
- **Real-estate disposition.** Station 1 (headquarters at 17000 Chagrin Boulevard) and Station 2 are City-owned; their disposition is a City Council matter independent of operational wind-down.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**
- **Personal data (EMS patient records):** Retained per Ohio EMS retention rules and HIPAA; on successor transition, transferred under a HIPAA-compliant business-associate or successor-covered-entity arrangement; otherwise archived to the City under records-management law.
- **Institutional records (fire incident reports, NFIRS data):** Retained per Ohio municipal records retention schedule; archived to City clerk.
- **Derived outputs (citations issued, abatement orders, occupancy condemnations):** Transferred to successor code-enforcement authority; existing orders remain effective until reissued or rescinded by successor under R63 and R65.
- **Audit logs (dispatch logs, ePCR audit trails):** Retained per ePCR vendor and CECOMS retention schedules; preserved for any open litigation or regulatory matter.

**9.5 — Downstream effect reversibility:**
The Department's outputs propagated to other systems include: hospital patient handoffs (information flows into hospital records, where it persists independent of SHFD); EMS billing records propagated to insurers; fire-incident reports propagated to insurance underwriters (R63 concern); abatement orders propagated to building, housing, and zoning authorities; suspected-arson reports propagated to State Fire Marshal and law enforcement. On termination, reversal mechanisms are: hospital records remain under the hospital's authority and are not invalidated by SHFD dissolution; abatement-order reversal requires successor action; insurance-effect reversal is not within the Department's authority to compel.

**9.6 — Replacement feasibility:**
The Department could be replaced through (a) merger with a neighboring department, (b) contract with a regional fire authority, or (c) a new combined district. Each option carries its own structural reorganization and is the subject of the regional consolidation conversations periodically raised in the eastern Cuyahoga County municipalities. Replacement is feasible but not trivial.

**9.7 — Reversibility verification:**
The declared 18-month timeline is estimated, not verified. ANNOTATION: SHFD has not, in publicly available materials, rehearsed dismantling or simulated shutdown. Per R43, the §9.1 declaration is treated as estimated.
