## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Regional Income Tax Agency (RITA). The Agency is the operational arm of the Regional Council of Governments (RCOG) formed under Ohio Revised Code Section 167.

**1.2 — Functional description:**
RITA is a political subdivision of the State of Ohio formed in 1971 by thirty-eight Ohio municipalities under ORC §167 (Regional Councils of Governments). The Agency administers, collects, and enforces the municipal income tax ordinances of approximately 400 Ohio cities, villages, and joint economic development districts that have joined the RCOG as members. RITA functions include:
- **Registration** of individual and business taxpayers with a tax-filing obligation in any member municipality.
- **Receipt and processing of tax returns**, including individual returns (Form 37), employer withholding returns (Form 11 series), W-2 reconciliations (Form 17), net-profit returns (Form 27), and declarations of exemption.
- **Collection** of municipal income tax through employer withholding, estimated payments, return-with-payment, and post-assessment collection.
- **Distribution** of collected tax (less the Agency's cost of collections) to each member municipality based on taxpayer-allocated work-city and resident-city information.
- **Audit, compliance review, and non-filer identification** including federal-tax-information (FTI) match comparison with IRS data under information-sharing agreements.
- **Assessment** of additional tax, penalties, and interest under the standardized framework of ORC Chapter 718 (Municipal Income Taxes), Ohio's comprehensive statutory framework for municipal income taxation.
- **Billing and notice issuance** to taxpayers identified as having an unmet obligation.
- **Payment-plan administration** for taxpayers unable to pay in full.
- **Refund processing** and credit reconciliation for taxpayers who have overpaid.
- **Subpoena issuance** for non-filers and for compliance review.
- **Litigation** in the appropriate municipal, county, or state court for collection of delinquent tax, including civil action against taxpayers and employers; legal action is conducted in the name of the member municipality whose tax is at issue, with RITA representation.
- **Administrative appeal facilitation** to each member municipality's Local Board of Tax Review under ORC §718.11 (every taxing municipality must maintain a Local Board of Tax Review independent of the tax administrator).
- **Bulk tax filing and Modernized eFile (MeF)** infrastructure for professional preparers and large employers.
- **Information sharing and reciprocal credit calculation** with the City of Cleveland, the Central Collection Agency (CCA, which collects for Cleveland and certain other municipalities), and other Ohio municipal-tax authorities, for the credit-for-tax-paid-to-another-municipality system under ORC §718.04.

RITA does not levy tax — the levy is by each member municipality through its own enacted ordinance. RITA's Board of Trustees is authorized to administer and enforce the income tax laws of each participating municipality; however, each municipality retains its concurrent right to administer and enforce its own ordinance.

[x] Both — describe:
RITA exercises **direct** authority over taxpayers through assessment, billing, audit, subpoena, collection action, refund denial, and litigation initiation (in the name of the member municipality). RITA exercises **indirect** authority by (a) interpreting and applying each member municipality's income-tax ordinance through administrative practice, with interpretive choices that materially shape outcomes across hundreds of jurisdictions; (b) operating as the administrative pipeline through which Ohio Revised Code Chapter 718's standardized provisions are made operational at the local level for more than half of Ohio's municipalities with an income tax; (c) producing tax forms, instructions, and interpretive guidance that effectively define the compliance posture for participating municipalities. Under R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition), the interpretive-application function is itself authority for compliance purposes.

**1.5 — Statutory or constitutional basis:**
- **Ohio Revised Code Section 167** (Regional Councils of Governments) — establishes the RCOG legal form under which RITA is organized.
- **Ohio Revised Code Chapter 718** (Municipal Income Taxes) — the comprehensive state framework standardizing municipal income tax administration in Ohio, including:
  - §718.01 (definitions and uniform tax base);
  - §718.03 (employer withholding);
  - §718.04 (credit for taxes paid to other municipalities);
  - §718.05 (return filing requirements);
  - §718.11 (Local Board of Tax Review — mandatory in each taxing municipality, separate from RITA);
  - §718.27 (interest and penalty standardization);
  - §718.36 (audits);
  - §718.85 et seq. (centralized administration of business net-profit taxes through the Ohio Department of Taxation — a state-level alternative to RITA for business net-profit tax).
- **Ohio Revised Code Chapter 5747** (Personal Income Tax) — context for the state-municipal interaction; not RITA's direct authority.
- **Ohio Revised Code Chapter 1716** (Charitable Trust Supervision) — not directly applicable to RITA but governs the state's general supervision of public entities.
- **Each member municipality's codified ordinances** establishing that municipality's income tax — RITA's authority within each jurisdiction is co-extensive with that municipality's ordinance and is bounded by the standardized ORC Chapter 718 framework.
- **RCOG agreement** (the constitutive document of the Regional Council of Governments that established and operates RITA), under which member municipalities elect the nine-member Board of Trustees for three-year terms.
- **Ohio Revised Code §149.43** (Public Records Act) and **§121.22** (Open Meetings Act) — RITA is a political subdivision subject to Ohio sunshine laws.
- **Internal Revenue Code §6103** and Federal Information Sharing Agreements with the IRS governing Federal Tax Information (FTI) handling.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 — Geographic reach:**
[x] Multi-municipality — approximately 400 Ohio cities, villages, and joint economic development districts. RITA's authority surface is the aggregate of each member municipality's geographic jurisdiction. RITA does not exercise authority in any non-member Ohio municipality (including Columbus, Cleveland, and CCA-member municipalities such as those in the Akron-area CCA grouping). RITA does not exercise authority outside Ohio.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest actions RITA can take against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Assessment of tax, penalty, and interest** under ORC Chapter 718's standardized framework. Penalty rates and interest rates are standardized by §718.27 across all Ohio municipal income tax administration.
- **Subpoena power** for non-filers and for audit-and-compliance review.
- **Lien against real and personal property** through court action securing delinquent tax balance; the lien is in the name of the member municipality whose tax is at issue.
- **Garnishment of wages** through court order in collection action.
- **Bank-account levy** through court order.
- **Civil litigation** in municipal, county, or state court for collection of delinquent tax.
- **Audit and examination** of taxpayer books, records, and tax-relevant documents under §718.36.
- **Reciprocal credit denial** when supporting documentation is not provided.
- **Refund denial or offset** against other unpaid tax liabilities.
- **Information-sharing with the IRS** through the FTI program for non-filer identification and for compliance review.
- **Referral to municipal prosecutor** for criminal prosecution of willful failure to file, failure to pay over withheld taxes, and similar offenses under each municipality's ordinance.

RITA does not exercise law-enforcement arrest authority. RITA cannot, by its own action, change the tax rate of any member municipality (each municipality's council retains that authority by ordinance). RITA cannot impose a tax in a non-member municipality. RITA cannot, by its own action, change the substantive provisions of ORC Chapter 718 — those are state-statutory and subject to amendment by the Ohio General Assembly.

**3.3 — Resource ceiling:**
- **Maximum operating budget:** RITA reports a 2025 average cost of collections of 1.04% of revenue collected. With more than $2 billion in annual municipal income tax revenue collected and distributed, the Agency's operating budget is in the $20–25 million range as a structural estimate. ANNOTATION: a reviewer with access to RITA's published Comprehensive Annual Financial Report (CAFR/ACFR) can populate the exact figure.
- **Maximum staff (FTE):** UNKNOWN specifically from the public-facing materials reviewed; the Agency operates offices in Brecksville (headquarters), central Ohio (formerly Upper Arlington/Worthington area), and Youngstown, plus distributed remote-and-hybrid positions.
- **Maximum data systems and data volume:** Includes the proprietary tax-administration system supporting MyAccount, FastFile, FastPay, FastReg, and document upload; the Bulk Tax Filing program for large filers; Modernized eFile (MeF) integration with commercial tax-preparation software; the FTI handling environment under IRS Publication 1075 security requirements; the public website infrastructure.

**3.4 — Explicit exclusions — what this authority may NOT do:**
[x] Geographic exclusions: RITA may not exercise authority in any non-member Ohio municipality. RITA may not extend authority outside Ohio's geographic boundaries.
[x] Action exclusions: RITA may not levy a tax — only each member municipality, through its council, may do so by ordinance. RITA may not exceed the ORC Chapter 718 standardized framework for penalty, interest, audit, and procedural matters. RITA may not impose collection actions inconsistent with the member municipality's ordinance. RITA may not retaliate against taxpayers who file appeals or complaints. RITA may not arrest, detain, or imprison a taxpayer; criminal prosecution authority rests with the municipal prosecutor.
[x] Data exclusions: RITA may not disclose taxpayer information except as permitted by ORC §718.13 (confidentiality), ORC §149.43 (Public Records Act, with applicable taxpayer-confidentiality exemptions), federal information-sharing agreements, and court orders. RITA may not share Federal Tax Information outside the constraints of IRC §6103 and IRS Publication 1075. RITA may not retain individually identifiable tax information beyond the periods required by retention schedules and ORC retention requirements.
[x] Procedural exclusions: RITA may not deny a taxpayer access to the Local Board of Tax Review of the member municipality whose tax is at issue (ORC §718.11). RITA may not bypass the appellate framework set forth in ORC §5717.011 (appeals from Local Board of Tax Review decisions to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals). RITA may not act in violation of the Open Meetings Act for Board of Trustees and RCOG meetings.
[x] Temporal exclusions: Statutory limitations periods under ORC §718.12 constrain the period during which RITA may assess additional tax (generally three years from the filing date or due date, with extensions for fraud, failure to file, and certain other circumstances).
[x] Other exclusions: RITA may not impose conditions on credit-for-tax-paid-to-another-municipality beyond those authorized by ORC §718.04 and the member municipality's credit-rate ordinance. RITA may not act in a manner inconsistent with its fiduciary duty to its member municipalities under the RCOG agreement. Board of Trustees members and RITA personnel must recuse from matters in which they have a personal financial interest under ORC Chapter 102 (Ohio Ethics Law).

**3.5 — Indirect influence boundaries:**
RITA's indirect-authority outputs include:
- **Form design, instructions, and interpretive guidance.** Used by: taxpayers across approximately 400 municipalities; tax-preparation professionals; commercial-software vendors integrating with RITA's MeF system. Decision shaped: how Ohio municipal income tax obligations are operationally determined and reported.
- **Administrative interpretation of ORC Chapter 718** provisions. Used by: member municipalities (which generally follow RITA's interpretive lead); the Ohio municipal-tax administrator community. Decision shaped: practical scope of municipal income tax across more than half of Ohio's taxing jurisdictions.
- **Bulk Tax Filing and MeF integration.** Used by: large employers, payroll-processing companies, commercial tax-software vendors. Decision shaped: how Ohio municipal income tax is automated and operationalized within enterprise payroll and tax-compliance systems.
- **Information sharing with the City of Cleveland, the Central Collection Agency, the Ohio Department of Taxation, and the IRS.** Used by: each receiving authority. Decision shaped: each authority's own enforcement targeting.
- **Local Board of Tax Review case patterns.** Used by: each member municipality's Local Board of Tax Review (which decides taxpayer appeals from RITA assessments). Decision shaped: practical scope of administrative discretion.
- **Speakers Bureau, tax seminars, and annual webinar.** Decision shaped: tax-preparation professional practice and employer compliance.

**3.6 — Anti-proxy declaration:**
The framework concern most relevant to RITA is whether operational tools are used to achieve effects that the underlying statute or ordinance does not directly authorize. ANNOTATION: Public sources do not show an explicit anti-proxy commitment. Practical anti-proxy concerns include: using federal-tax-information matching to identify non-filers in ways that disproportionately target lower-income filers whose IRS records are easier to match against state-level information; using audit-prioritization heuristics that produce concentrated enforcement against specific occupational or geographic subgroups; using collection-action sequencing that produces aggregate effects on vulnerable populations more pronounced than the underlying tax obligation would suggest; using interpretive choices on residency-and-allocation questions that systematically favor revenue maximization over taxpayer-favorable readings.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Local Board of Tax Review and the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals.**
- Who can activate it: Any taxpayer aggrieved by a RITA assessment, denial of refund, or other adverse determination may appeal to the Local Board of Tax Review of the member municipality whose tax is at issue (ORC §718.11). Decisions of the Local Board of Tax Review are appealable to the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals under ORC §5717.011. Decisions of the Board of Tax Appeals are appealable to the Ohio Court of Appeals or directly to the Ohio Supreme Court under ORC §5717.04.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on filing within statutory windows (generally 60 days from notice of assessment for Local Board appeal; 60 days from Local Board decision for Board of Tax Appeals).
- What "stopped" means: A specific assessment is reversed, modified, or remanded; in structural-pattern cases, the Ohio Board of Tax Appeals or appellate courts may issue rulings that bind RITA's future practice across all member municipalities.
- Independence justification: Each Local Board of Tax Review is statutorily required by ORC §718.11 to be independent of the tax administrator (RITA cannot serve on or staff the Board). The Ohio Board of Tax Appeals is a state-level quasi-judicial body whose members are appointed by the Governor with Senate confirmation, independent of RITA and of municipal tax administrators. Ohio appellate courts are constitutionally separate.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ohio Administrative Procedure Act for Board of Tax Appeals proceedings; ordinary appellate process for court review.

**STOP PATH 2 — Member-municipality concurrent authority; RCOG governance.**
- Who can activate it: (a) Any member municipality, through its concurrent right to administer and enforce its own income tax law (as expressly preserved in RITA's structure), may decline RITA's interpretive lead in a specific matter or may exit the RCOG entirely; (b) the RCOG itself, through Board of Trustees action elected by member municipalities for three-year terms, may direct RITA's Executive Director, replace the Executive Director, or restructure Agency operations; (c) the Ohio General Assembly may amend ORC §167 and ORC Chapter 718 with restructuring effect.
- Trigger condition: Member-municipality action (an exit decision); Board of Trustees action; legislative action.
- What "stopped" means: A specific RITA interpretation reversed for a specific municipality; the Executive Director replaced; Agency operations restructured; in the limit case, a member municipality exits RITA and resumes direct or alternative-administrator collection.
- Independence justification: Member municipalities are independent local governments not employed by or financially dependent on RITA in the framework's structural sense; municipalities' financial relationship to RITA is fee-for-service (RITA charges a cost-of-collections percentage to remit on collections). The Ohio General Assembly is constitutionally separate.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Member-municipality governance process; RCOG governance process; ordinary legislative process.

**STOP PATH 3 — Federal information-sharing oversight; Ohio Auditor of State; private litigation.**
- Who can activate it: The Internal Revenue Service Safeguards Program, which audits state and local agencies receiving Federal Tax Information for compliance with IRC §6103 and IRS Publication 1075 — adverse findings can result in suspension of FTI access; the Ohio Auditor of State, who conducts financial-and-compliance audits of political subdivisions including RITA (RITA's CAFR has been awarded GFOA Certificates of Achievement for Excellence in Financial Reporting, indicating regular external-audit review); private taxpayers, through civil litigation including class actions (for example, the Ohio Supreme Court case *Schaad v. Alder* and related challenges to RITA practice have surfaced over the years); the U.S. Department of Justice, in extreme circumstances involving federal-civil-rights or federal-tax-information misuse.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on scheduled federal-and-state-audit cycles; complaint-driven for litigation.
- What "stopped" means: Audit findings producing corrective action plans; FTI-access suspension (which would materially compromise RITA's non-filer-identification capability); civil judgments establishing precedent; settlement-and-consent-decree arrangements.
- Independence justification: The IRS, the Ohio Auditor of State, federal courts, and private litigants are structurally separate from RITA. The Ohio Auditor of State is constitutionally elected and reports to Ohio voters, not to RITA.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Federal-and-state-administrative-procedure processes; ordinary appellate process for litigation.

**5.4 — Penalty for activating stop:**
