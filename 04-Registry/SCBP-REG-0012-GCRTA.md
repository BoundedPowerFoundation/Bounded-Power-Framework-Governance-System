# Mechanism Record — Greater Cleveland Regional Transit Authority

## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Greater Cleveland Regional Transit Authority (officially GCRTA, locally referred to as the RTA).

**1.2 — Functional description:**
The authority is the public transit agency serving Cuyahoga County, Ohio (59 communities, approximately 1.2 million residents). GCRTA operates approximately 40 bus routes, three rail lines (Red Line heavy rail; Blue, Green, and Waterfront light rail lines), three bus rapid transit (BRT) lines (HealthLine, Cleveland State Line, and others), and ADA-mandated paratransit service. Total rail network is approximately 33 miles. Annual ridership approximately 25 million (21 million bus, 4 million rail per 2025 data). The authority operates the GCRTA Transit Police Department, a sworn law-enforcement agency with arrest authority covering transit property, transit vehicles, and fare enforcement. The authority levies and collects a 1% Cuyahoga County sales tax (since 1975, approved by voter referendum) which funds approximately 76.6% of the 2026 operating budget. The authority issues fare and pass products through the EZfare system, administers Commuter Advantage programs, conducts contracting for transit-related services (vehicles, fuel, infrastructure, paratransit contractors), operates transit centers and stations, manages real estate holdings adjacent to stations, and participates in regional planning through the Northeast Ohio Areawide Coordinating Agency (NOACA).

**1.3 — Authority classification:**

**1.5 — Statutory or constitutional basis:**
- Ohio Revised Code Chapter 306 (Regional Transit Authorities), authorizing the creation and operation of GCRTA
- ORC Chapter 5739 (Sales Tax), authorizing the 1% county sales tax that funds GCRTA
- ORC Chapter 4581 governing certain transit-police functions; ORC Title 29 (criminal law) and Title 25 (courts) applicable to Transit Police authority
- Federal Transit Administration (FTA) rules under 49 U.S.C. §5302 et seq.
- Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (paratransit requirement); 49 CFR Parts 27, 37, 38, 39
- Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (FTA Title VI Circular 4702.1B applies)
- National Transit Database (NTD) reporting requirements (49 U.S.C. §5335)
- GCRTA Code of Regulations (Code Book), revised on three-year cycle; most recent revisions in 2025 include updates required by federal executive orders affecting DEI language
- Collective bargaining agreements with the Amalgamated Transit Union (ATU) Local 268 and the Fraternal Order of Police Ohio Labor Council (FOP)

ANNOTATION: GCRTA was established December 30, 1974, with operational control over the Cleveland Transit System and Shaker Heights Rapid Transit assumed September 5, 1975. The voter-approved 1% sales tax was approved July 22, 1975, and has not been changed since.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — GCRTA Civilian Oversight Committee (COC) and Internal Audit**
- Who can activate it: any complainant who is not a GCRTA employee (employees use internal HR processes) may file a complaint against Transit Police employees through the COC. The Executive Director of Internal Audit (per public sources, a position separate from operational management) provides internal review.
- Trigger condition: filing of public complaint regarding alleged Transit Police misconduct (specific filing criteria per COC Operating Procedures)
- What "stopped" means: the COC makes findings and recommendations to the Chief of Police regarding investigation outcomes and proposed discipline. ANNOTATION: per the COC Operating Procedures and public sources, COC authority is *advisory* — it makes recommendations, not binding determinations. The Chief of Police is the deciding authority; the Chief or designee determines which complaints are heard by COC versus handled by internal HR processes. This is structurally narrower than "stop authority" in the framework's sense.
- Independence justification: COC members are non-Transit-Police personnel; the COC is composed of seven members including at least one retired OPOTA-trained police officer. Members receive $1,800 annual compensation paid from the Board of Trustees' departmental budget.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: per COC Operating Procedures (specific timelines for individual complaints UNKNOWN); meetings are monthly per public sources.

ANNOTATION: the COC is the most-detailed civilian oversight structure of any of the Cuyahoga County authorities reviewed in this registry. It exceeds the SHPD model. However, its advisory-only authority and the Chief of Police's gatekeeping role over which complaints reach the COC are framework-relevant limitations.

**STOP PATH 2 — Cuyahoga County Voters and GCRTA Board of Trustees**
- Who can activate it: Cuyahoga County voters through referendum on tax authorization (the 1% sales tax could be challenged or replaced by referendum); the GCRTA Board of Trustees (10 members appointed under ORC Chapter 306) through governance decisions
- Trigger condition: judgment-based, expressed through electoral action or Board legislative action
- What "stopped" means: voters could defeat any future tax measure (e.g., the proposed 0.5% sales tax increase discussed in public sources); Board could halt specific actions, modify policy, remove the CEO/General Manager, or restructure operations
- Independence justification: voters are independent of GCRTA; the Board is appointed by Cuyahoga County and member-municipalities, with appointing authorities outside GCRTA operations
- Post-stop review process and timeline: electoral cycles; Board operates on monthly meeting schedule

ANNOTATION: the Board appointment structure under ORC Chapter 306 includes appointments from Cuyahoga County, the City of Cleveland, and suburban communities. This creates structural independence from any single municipality's executive but does not create direct voter election of Board members. Voters' principal lever is through the sales tax referendum mechanism.

**STOP PATH 3 — Federal and State Oversight**
- Who can activate it: Federal Transit Administration (FTA); Ohio Department of Transportation (ODOT) including State Safety Oversight (SSO) functions; U.S. Department of Justice (Title VI, ADA enforcement); federal courts; state courts; National Transit Database compliance mechanisms; Ohio Auditor of State
- Trigger condition: threshold-based — federal statutory and regulatory standards; ADA paratransit requirements; Title VI requirements; rail-safety standards; federal grant conditions
- What "stopped" means: withholding of federal grants (which fund a material portion of capital and some operating costs); FTA imposition of conditions or oversight; SSO suspension of rail operations on safety grounds; judicial injunctive relief
- Independence justification: federal and state authorities operate outside GCRTA structure
- Post-stop review process and timeline: federal investigation timelines vary (typically multi-year for Title VI investigations and pattern-or-practice inquiries); SSO is real-time for imminent-hazard situations

**5.4 — Penalty for activating stop:**
- [x] Confirmed — no formal penalty for filing complaints with the COC, filing Title VI or ADA complaints with FTA/DOJ, or pursuing judicial action. The COC Operating Procedures provide explicit complainant-protection terms.

**5.5 — Stop-path independence verification:**
- [x] Yes — COC, voters, Board, federal and state authorities can act without GCRTA cooperation. The COC's advisory-only authority is a structural limitation on stop-path strength but not on independence.

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**
UNKNOWN. No public dismantling plan exists. Substantive components of a wind-down would include:
- Transition of ADA paratransit obligation to a successor entity (federal mandate persists regardless of GCRTA existence)
- Rail operations transfer (the federally-funded rail infrastructure cannot simply be abandoned; successor entity or transition arrangement required)
- Bus service transition (potentially to municipal operators or contracted private service)
- Resolution of federal grant compliance obligations
- Disposition of approximately 80 acres of real estate
- Resolution of capital debt and the Railcar Replacement Program order
- Transition of EZfare system participation
- Employee transition per CBA and applicable employment law
- Sales tax disposition

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- *Federal capital investment:* federal funding has been used for rail infrastructure, vehicles, and capital projects; FTA requirements bind transit infrastructure to transit use for defined periods
- *ADA paratransit federal mandate:* dismantling without successor leaves a federal compliance gap
- *County sales tax structure:* the 1% sales tax is tied to GCRTA existence; dismantling raises questions about disposition of the tax
- *Rail infrastructure:* 33-mile rail network is sunk infrastructure with very limited alternative uses
- *Real estate holdings:* approximately 80 acres adjacent to stations
- *Employee population:* 500–1,000 employees with CBA-protected employment rights
- *Regional dependency:* 1.2 million Cuyahoga County residents and 25 million annual rider-trips depend on operations
- *Pension obligations:* employee pension obligations under Ohio Public Employees Retirement System

R61 (Anti-Entrenchment) finding: GCRTA is structurally embedded in county infrastructure, federal compliance obligations, and regional transit dependency. Dismantling without replacement would create cascading effects across federal compliance, regional transit access, and county fiscal structure.
