## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Cuyahoga County Division of Children and Family Services (DCFS); also referenced in some materials as Cuyahoga County Department of Children and Family Services or CCDCFS. Following the 2023 reorganization under Cuyahoga County Ordinance O2023-0007, DCFS operates as a Division within the County Department of Health and Human Services.

**1.2 — Functional description:**
DCFS is the Public Children Services Agency (PCSA) for Cuyahoga County under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 5153, responsible by statute for the investigation and disposition of suspected child abuse and neglect within the county. Its functions include:
- **Intake and screening** of reports of suspected child abuse and neglect through the 24/7 216-696-KIDS hotline staffed by Child Protection Specialists, who document allegations, assess priority, and determine whether a report is screened in for investigation.
- **Investigation** of screened-in reports through either the traditional response or alternative response (differential response approach) under ORC §2151.011 and OAC Chapter 5101:2-36.
- **Safety planning** with families to maintain children in the home where safe, including in-home protective supervision.
- **Petition for removal** from parental custody when, in the Division's assessment, conditions warrant filing a complaint of abuse, neglect, or dependency under ORC Chapter 2151 in the Juvenile Division of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas. Removal authority flows through the court, not directly through the Division.
- **Emergency custody** under ORC §2151.31 — taking a child into custody pending a shelter-care hearing — exercisable by law enforcement at the Division's request when statutory criteria are met.
- **Temporary custody, legal custody, and permanent custody** of children when ordered by the Juvenile Court.
- **Foster care, kinship care, and adoptive placement** — recruitment, licensing, training, and matching of foster and adoptive caregivers under ORC Chapter 5103 and OAC rules; placement of children in DCFS custody.
- **Adoption services** — finalization of adoptions when permanent custody has been granted to DCFS.
- **Youth permanency and extended foster care** under ORC §2151.451 et seq. for young adults aging out of care, up to age 21.
- **Voluntary services** — short-term and extended family services for families where investigation does not result in a petition but services may prevent future maltreatment.
- **Mandatory reporting** as the statutory recipient of reports under ORC §2151.421.
- **Coordination** with law enforcement (under cross-reporting protocols), the Juvenile Court, the County Prosecutor, schools, medical providers, and community service providers.

DCFS may not, by its own authority, terminate parental rights — that authority lies exclusively with the Juvenile Court. DCFS recommends; the Court decides.

**1.3 — Authority classification:**
[ ] Direct
[ ] Indirect
[x] Both — describe:
DCFS exercises **direct** authority through intake screening decisions (which cases are investigated and which are not), investigation findings (substantiated/indicated/unsubstantiated), service-plan content, case-closing decisions, foster-and-adoptive home licensing decisions, and the day-to-day custodial care of children placed in DCFS custody. DCFS exercises **indirect** authority through its petitions and recommendations to the Juvenile Court, which materially shape — but do not bind — court orders affecting custody, visitation, services, and permanency. The Division's recommendation posture in a case is the practical input the Court most often accepts; R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition) treats this recommending function as authority for compliance purposes.

[ ] Future generations

ASSUMPTION: Approximately 1,265,000 — the resident population of Cuyahoga County as of the 2020 Census. **2.3 — Of the affected population, what number/proportion are non-consenting:**
ASSUMPTION: Approximately 1,265,000. Parents are non-consenting in the framework's sense as soon as a report concerning their family is screened in for investigation; their participation in investigation interviews, home visits, and safety planning operates under the structural understanding that refusal may itself contribute to an adverse-finding determination or to a court petition. Even families who voluntarily seek DCFS service do so within a structure where involuntary action is the alternative.

**2.4 — Vulnerable population specifics:**
[x] Children (under age of majority) — protective measures in place include: the Juvenile Court jurisdictional structure under ORC Chapter 2151, which interposes a court between DCFS recommendation and most consequential outcomes (removal, change of custody, termination of parental rights); the federal Adoption and Safe Families Act framework requiring "reasonable efforts" to prevent removal and to reunify; the Indian Child Welfare Act for Indian children; the Guardian ad Litem appointment under Juv.R. 4 and ORC §2151.281; court-appointed counsel for indigent parents and children; the statewide Youth Ombudsman Office at 1-877-OH-YOUTH for youth in care; the statewide Family Ombudsman Office at the same toll-free number; the Youth Navigator Network for youth up to age 21 currently or formerly in care.
[x] Patients during medical care — children in DCFS custody are protected by HIPAA, by informed-consent surrogate-decision frameworks, and by mandatory court approval for certain categories of treatment (psychiatric medication for children in care; surgery; abortion).
[x] Detained or confined persons — children in residential treatment, group homes, congregate-care settings, and (in some configurations) institutional placements under DCFS custody.
[x] Persons in coercive economic relationships — caregivers receiving foster-care maintenance payments whose continued receipt depends on continued certification by DCFS; kinship caregivers receiving kinship-care assistance whose continued receipt depends on cooperation with DCFS case planning.
[x] Persons unable to advocate for themselves due to disability, incapacity, or circumstance — infants and very young children; children with cognitive or developmental disabilities; parents with cognitive or developmental disabilities whose capacity to participate in case planning may be limited; non-English-speaking parents; parents who are themselves under DCFS history (former foster youth becoming parents).

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 — Geographic reach:**
[x] Single county — DCFS is the sole Public Children Services Agency for all of Cuyahoga County, including the City of Cleveland and the 58 other municipalities. Unlike public-health authority — which in Cuyahoga County is split between the Cleveland Department of Public Health and the Cuyahoga County Board of Health — child-protection authority is unified at the county level under ORC Chapter 5153. DCFS coordinates with adjacent-county PCSAs (Lake, Geauga, Portage, Summit, Medina, Lorain) on cross-jurisdictional cases under interstate-compact and inter-county-transfer rules.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest actions DCFS can take or initiate against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Emergency custody and removal** — under ORC §2151.31, when DCFS has reasonable cause to believe a child is in immediate danger of serious physical harm and circumstances do not permit obtaining an ex parte court order, DCFS may request that law enforcement take a child into protective custody. A shelter-care hearing must follow within statutory deadlines.
- **Petition for adjudication of abuse, neglect, or dependency** under ORC §2151.27, initiating Juvenile Court jurisdiction. If sustained, the Court may order protective supervision, temporary custody to DCFS or kin, or legal custody.
- **Petition for permanent custody** under ORC §2151.413, which if granted terminates parental rights and authorizes DCFS to consent to adoption.
- **Substantiated or indicated finding of abuse/neglect** on internal record (and on ODCY central registry in defined categories), with downstream consequences for the named alleged perpetrator's employment in child-serving fields, custody disputes, foster-licensing applications, and other contexts under R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) and R65 (Propagation Duty Requirement).
- **Denial, suspension, or revocation of foster-home certification** under ORC Chapter 5103 and OAC rules, ending the certified caregiver's ability to receive placements and foster-care payments.
- **Case plan content** for an open case — specifying services parents must participate in, behaviors they must change, and visitation conditions, with non-compliance feeding into the Court's permanency-decision framework.
- **Day-to-day custodial decisions** for children in DCFS custody — placement selection, education choices, medical consent, religious practice, contact with parents and siblings, recreational and travel decisions.
- **Mandatory reporting transmissions** to law enforcement and county prosecutor under cross-reporting protocols.
- **Subpoena power** through the County Prosecutor in litigation.

DCFS does not exercise law-enforcement arrest authority. DCFS does not directly terminate parental rights; only the Juvenile Court does. DCFS does not directly impose criminal penalties; only the courts do.

**3.3 — Resource ceiling:**
- **Maximum operating budget:** UNKNOWN as a specific recent figure. DCFS is funded through a complex mix of (a) Cuyahoga County Health and Human Services Levy, (b) federal Title IV-E and Title IV-B funding administered through ODCY, (c) federal Title XX Social Services Block Grant, (d) state subsidy and state-allocated TANF, (e) Medicaid reimbursement for certain services, (f) federal CAPTA (Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act) grant, (g) other federal and state grants. ANNOTATION: A reviewer with access to the County HHS adopted budget should populate this figure. Public reporting from 2018 referenced approximately 41 hotline screening workers among a larger investigative and case-management staff.
- **Maximum staff (FTE):** UNKNOWN specifically. DCFS staffing is structured across Intake (hotline screening), Investigation (Child Protection Specialists), Ongoing Case Management, Foster Care and Adoption Services, Resources & Placement, and administrative-and-support functions.
- **Maximum data systems and data volume:** Includes the State Automated Child Welfare Information System (SACWIS — Ohio's version is administered through ODCY); the local case-management system; the ODCY central registry of substantiated abuse/neglect findings; provider-network and foster-home registries.

**3.4 — Explicit exclusions — what this authority may NOT do:**
[x] Geographic exclusions: DCFS may not exercise direct authority outside Cuyahoga County except by interstate compact (Interstate Compact on the Placement of Children, ICPC, codified at ORC §5103.20) or inter-county transfer under Juvenile Court rules.
[x] Action exclusions: DCFS may not terminate parental rights by its own action. DCFS may not place a child in adoptive placement without Court order conveying permanent custody. DCFS may not conduct law-enforcement searches except as authorized by statute or by the consent of the parent or guardian. DCFS may not impose criminal penalties. DCFS may not remove a child from parental custody for more than the statutory shelter-care window without a sustained court order.
[x] Data exclusions: DCFS may not disclose case-record information except as permitted by ORC §5153.17 (confidentiality of children-services records), HIPAA, FERPA, and applicable court orders. DCFS may not disclose records of unsubstantiated reports to the named alleged perpetrator's employer or to the public except in defined statutory circumstances. DCFS may not retain reports beyond the periods required by ODCY-prescribed retention schedules.
[x] Procedural exclusions: DCFS may not act in violation of the Open Meetings Act for board and advisory-board meetings; may not adopt or amend program rules without following applicable administrative-procedure protocols; may not deny services on the basis of any protected classification.
[x] Temporal exclusions: Statutory permanency timelines under ORC §2151.413 and ASFA constrain the time DCFS may hold children in temporary custody before petitioning for permanency (the "twelve of twenty-two months" framework and related provisions).
[x] Other exclusions: DCFS may not retaliate against mandated reporters or against parties who file complaints with the Ohio Department of Children and Youth, the Youth Ombudsman, or the Family Ombudsman. DCFS may not consider a parent's lawful exercise of constitutional rights (religious practice within statutory child-safety limits; political speech; refusal to admit officers without a warrant absent exigency) as in itself evidence of abuse or neglect.

**3.5 — Indirect influence boundaries:**
DCFS's indirect-authority outputs include:
- **Petitions and recommendations to the Juvenile Court.** Used by: Juvenile Court judges and magistrates. Decision shaped: every consequential custody, visitation, services, and permanency order in DCFS-involved cases.
- **Central-registry transmissions to ODCY.** Used by: state ODCY; downstream employer background-check systems for child-serving fields; out-of-state PCSAs receiving ICPC requests. Decision shaped: employability in child-serving fields, fitness assessments in custody disputes, foster-licensing eligibility.
- **Cross-reports to law enforcement and the County Prosecutor.** Used by: police; prosecutor; grand jury. Decision shaped: criminal investigation and charging decisions on the same underlying conduct.
- **Service-plan referrals to community providers.** Used by: parents and caregivers (who must participate in plan-specified services). Decision shaped: continuation or termination of the case; ultimately, custody outcome.
- **Foster-home certification and supervision.** Used by: foster caregivers (whose continued licensure depends on DCFS evaluation). Decision shaped: receipt of placements, foster-care payments, and continued participation in foster-and-adoption systems.
- **Adoption-finalization recommendations** to the Juvenile or Probate Court.
- **Communications with schools, medical providers, and other systems** under cross-system protocols.

**3.6 — Anti-proxy declaration:**
The framework concern most relevant to DCFS is whether decision criteria are used to achieve effects that the underlying statute does not directly authorize the Division to impose. ANNOTATION: Public sources do not show an explicit anti-proxy commitment of this kind. Practical anti-proxy concerns include: differential investigation intensity by family race, ethnicity, neighborhood, family-of-origin DCFS history, or other characteristic; using case-plan condition-stacking to make reunification practically unachievable in cases where the Division has decided permanent custody is the preferred outcome; using the "alternative response" track to extend agency contact with families without the procedural protections that a formal investigation provides; using mandatory-reporter feedback patterns to shape future reporting in ways that produce reporting concentration aligned with prior agency posture.

*Rules addressed: R04 (Functional Effect), R35 (Scope Narrowing Default), R55 (Fragmentation), R66 (Anti-Proxy), R74 (Proxy Equivalence Expansion). SCBP-09 §VII (Geographic Specificity), §VIII (Coercive Ceiling Specificity).*

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**
DCFS authority operates over bodily-harm subject matter in two distinct directions: (a) preventing harm to children through investigation and intervention; (b) imposing constraints on children's bodies and persons through removal, placement, and custodial decisions. Excess harm thresholds attach to both.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the rate of serious physical harm to children with DCFS-screened-in cases within 12 months of case closure exceeds the rolling three-year baseline by more than 25%, an internal-and-external structural review of intake-and-screening protocol is initiated. ANNOTATION: This is the framework analog of the public-child-fatality concern that surfaced in the 2018 Aniya Day-Garrett case and similar incidents nationally; specific Cuyahoga County metrics are not publicly aggregated as such.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the rate of serious physical harm to children **in DCFS custody** (in foster care, kinship care, or congregate care) exceeds peer-PCSA benchmarks for comparable urban county agencies by more than 25%, an automatic structural review of placement-and-supervision protocols is initiated.

Threshold: If a removal-with-emergency-custody order is shown on the record to have been issued without the procedural support required by ORC §2151.31 (reasonable cause; immediacy; subsequent prompt shelter-care hearing), the matter is reported under §11 and the removal protocol is reviewed.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, more than 5% of DCFS-initiated removal orders are reversed at shelter-care hearing or on subsequent juvenile-court review for procedural deficiency or for lack of factual support, the removal-petition protocol is reviewed.

[ ] Not applicable

**4.2 — Agency impairment threshold:**
DCFS authority is exercised through processes that materially restrict the agency of parents, children, and kin: investigation interviews, home visits, service plans, visitation conditions, placement decisions. Excess thresholds attach to procedural failure.

Threshold: If a parent is denied timely meaningful interpretation services during investigation interviews or court proceedings, the case is reported under §11.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Juvenile Court of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas.**
- Who can activate it: Parents, children (through Guardian ad Litem and counsel), kin, and other parties with standing in any open case. The Juvenile Court is interposed between DCFS recommendation and most consequential outcomes — removal, adjudication, disposition, permanency, termination of parental rights — and reviews DCFS positions at each step under statutory standards. Direct appeals lie to the Ohio Eighth District Court of Appeals.
- Trigger condition: Statutory triggers (shelter-care hearings within statutory windows after emergency removal; adjudicatory hearings within 30 days of complaint filing; dispositional hearings; semiannual case-plan review; annual permanency review).
- What "stopped" means: A removal is reversed at shelter-care hearing; a complaint is dismissed at adjudication; a case-plan provision is modified by court order; a permanency goal is rejected; a termination petition is denied. In rare cases, the Court may sanction DCFS for procedural violations.
- Independence justification: The Court of Common Pleas is constitutionally separate from county executive government. The six Juvenile Court judges are elected. The Court is not funded from DCFS revenue. Court rules of evidence and due-process protections apply to all consequential proceedings.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Appeal to the Ohio Eighth District Court of Appeals within 30 days; discretionary review by the Ohio Supreme Court.

**STOP PATH 2 — Ohio Department of Children and Youth (ODCY) supervisory authority and the Youth/Family Ombudsman Office.**

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 — Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):**

*Dependency A — Foster caregivers, kinship caregivers, and certified foster homes.* Function: day-to-day custodial care of children in DCFS custody. The framework treats foster caregivers as operational extensions of DCFS authority — they exercise daily supervision, discipline, education advocacy, and medical-decision support for DCFS-custodied children under DCFS oversight. Compensation structure: per-diem foster-care maintenance payments under federal Title IV-E and state schedules; kinship-care assistance under ORC §5101.852.

*Dependency B — Contracted service providers* (counseling agencies, parenting classes, substance-use treatment providers, mental-health providers, drug-testing labs, supervised-visitation centers, residential-treatment facilities) whose services are mandated in case plans and whose participation reports feed back into DCFS case decisions. Compensation structure: per-service contract rates and provider-network agreements through County contracting.

*Dependency C — Private child placing agencies (PCPAs)* and private residential providers. Function: under contract, certified to accept temporary, permanent, or legal custody of DCFS children and to place them in foster or adoptive homes; provide residential treatment for children with high-acuity needs. Compensation structure: per-diem placement rates substantially higher than DCFS-direct foster-care payments.

*Dependency D — Cuyahoga County Prosecutor's Office, Civil Division.* Function: legal representation of DCFS in juvenile-court proceedings; drafting of petitions, motions, and appeals; representation through trial and appellate court. Compensation structure: not a contracted relationship; the Prosecutor's Office is a separate elected office whose attorneys serve DCFS as a client agency.

**6.2 — Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with a structural relationship):**

- **Juvenile Division of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas.** Downstream — the principal adjudicator of DCFS petitions, recommendations, and case-plan content.
- **Ohio Department of Children and Youth (ODCY).** Upstream — sets the rules, administers federal pass-through funding, conducts oversight; the recently created (effective July 1, 2024 under HB 33) state-level authority for child-protection and youth services, formerly part of ODJFS.
- **Cuyahoga County Executive and County Department of Health and Human Services.** Upstream and internal — DCFS is a Division of HHS; the HHS Director reports to the County Executive; the Division Director reports to the HHS Director.
- **Cuyahoga County Council.** Upstream — appropriates the HHS Levy and reviews HHS budget; confirms certain HHS-related appointments.
- **DCFS Advisory Board** (ORC §5153.05). Lateral — advisory only, no decision-making authority; provides community-stakeholder feedback.
- **Statewide Youth Ombudsman Office and Family Ombudsman Office** (1-877-OH-YOUTH). Downstream lateral — receives concerns about county PCSA practice.
- **U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Administration for Children and Families (ACF).** Upstream — federal funder; conducts Title IV-E eligibility review and Child and Family Services Review (CFSR).
- **Adjacent-county PCSAs** (Lake, Geauga, Portage, Summit, Medina, Lorain) and out-of-state PCSAs through ICPC. Lateral — coordination on cross-jurisdictional placements.

**6.3 — Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure whose failure would compromise stop paths or auditability):**

- **State Automated Child Welfare Information System (SACWIS / Ohio's successor system).** If unavailable, contemporaneous case-record documentation on which judicial review and federal oversight depend is compromised.
- **ODCY central registry.** Permanent durable record of substantiated abuse-and-neglect findings; if lost or compromised, due-process challenges to entries become harder to support.
- **DCFS hotline call-recording and intake-documentation system.** If lost, the initial-screening record on which downstream investigation decisions rest is compromised, and adversarial-exposure review under R81 is impaired.
- **Court records system (Juvenile Division of the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas).** If lost, the record of judicial proceedings on which appellate review depends is compromised.

Five structural risks are visible from public sources:

**(a) Title IV-E funding-stream-driven service-mix bias.** Federal Title IV-E (foster care) and Title IV-B (family preservation) operate on different reimbursement formulas. The Family First Prevention Services Act (FFPSA, enacted 2018) was specifically designed to address the longstanding pattern in which federal financing favored removal-and-placement over in-home services. **(b) Private child placing agency and residential provider concentration.** Multiple PCPAs and residential providers contract with DCFS for placement services at per-diem rates substantially higher than DCFS-direct foster care. ANNOTATION: This is the pattern of every large U.S. urban county child-welfare system, not a deviation specific to Cuyahoga County.

**(c) Public-fatality-driven enforcement-posture oscillation.** Public child fatalities in DCFS-known cases (the 2018 Aniya Day-Garrett case is publicly documented; others have occurred) produce predictable patterns: heightened removal rates in the immediate aftermath; legislative attention; revised intake-and-investigation guidelines; subsequent oscillation as removal-rate increases produce their own structural-harm patterns. **(d) Mandatory-reporter feedback loops and demographic concentration.** Mandatory reporters (teachers, healthcare providers, child-care workers, police) receive feedback from DCFS about which reports were screened in, substantiated, or led to outcomes. Reporting patterns shift in response. This produces concentration in mandatory-reporter behavior — and downstream concentration of agency contact — that is correlated with demographic factors. R50 (Capture Risk Monitoring) attaches.

**(e) Foster-caregiver economic dependency.** Foster caregivers who depend on foster-care maintenance payments for household financial stability may be structurally unable to advocate against agency practice concerning the children placed with them, since complaint may risk continued certification. R69 (Burden of Proof Reversal) attaches structurally.

ANNOTATION: None of these is a deviation from how comparable large urban county PCSAs operate. Their identification is structural disclosure under R39, not a finding of misconduct.

## SECTION 8 — How is this authority watched?

**8.1 — Drift monitoring mechanism:**
- **Federal Child and Family Services Review (CFSR).** Multi-year federal review of state child-welfare systems with county-level data inputs.
- **Title IV-E Eligibility Reviews.** Federal periodic review of foster-care funding eligibility.
- **ODCY annual oversight and licensing reviews.**
- **DCFS Advisory Board.** Advisory feedback channel under ORC §5153.05.
- **Statewide Youth Ombudsman and Family Ombudsman Offices.** Complaint-driven independent review.
- **County Executive supervisory authority** through the HHS Director.
- **County Council budget and policy review.**
- **News-media and advocacy-organization monitoring** — particularly intense following publicly-reported child fatalities.

These collectively meet R41 (Institutional Drift Monitoring) at the organizational level. What is less developed in publicly available materials is granular decision-pattern monitoring at the case level (e.g., disaggregated decision-pattern publication by race, neighborhood, reporter type).

**8.2 — Capture risk monitoring:**
No specific public capture-risk monitoring mechanism beyond standard ethics-disclosure requirements is visible. The five capture-risk concerns surfaced in §6.4 are addressed only obliquely through federal CFSR and state oversight; no aggregate public report of funding-stream-bias indicators, provider concentration, fatality-cycle posture oscillation, mandatory-reporter feedback patterns, or caregiver-economic-dependency effects appears in publicly available materials reviewed.

**8.3 — Audit interfaces:**
- **Proactively published:** General agency-mission descriptions, policies-and-procedures summaries, aggregate annual reports (partial), Advisory Board meeting agendas and minutes, federal CFSR results.
- **Available on request:** Records under the Ohio Public Records Act (ORC §149.43), subject to extensive child-protection-specific exemptions under ORC §5153.17. Parties to a case may obtain their own case records subject to redactions.
- **Restricted:** Children-services case records (ORC §5153.17); central-registry entries (ORC §3109.412 and OAC); HIPAA-protected health information; FERPA-protected educational information; foster-home applicant records to a substantial degree; juvenile-court records restricted under Juv.R. and ORC §2151.18.

**8.4 — Adversarial exposure (R81):**
An external party with no insider access can evaluate DCFS's:
- Scope (yes — ORC Chapter 5153, ORC Chapter 2151, federal statute).
- Effect (partially — federal CFSR aggregate data is published; granular pattern analysis requires records-request-based assembly or research partnerships).
- Harm thresholds (no — none publicly declared in the framework's sense).
- Dependencies (partially — major statutory and budgetary dependencies visible; specific provider-contract terms require records requests).
- Reversibility (partially — statutory framework for some reversibility exists; permanent custody and adoption are designed to be permanent).
- Stop capability (yes — through juvenile-court process, administrative appeal, federal and state oversight, legislative action).

The principal R81 gaps are: granular decision-pattern publication; capture-risk monitoring product; explicit racial-and-ethnic-disparity tracking and disclosure.

**8.5 — Known structural failure modes:**
Failure modes observed in similar large urban county PCSAs and documented in child-welfare research and oversight literature:
- **Public-fatality cycle dynamics** — increased removal rates following publicized fatalities, with downstream over-removal effects; subsequent return to baseline producing under-protection effects; the cycle is national, not Cuyahoga-specific.
- **Racial-and-ethnic disproportionality** — over-representation of Black, Indigenous, and other-children-of-color in agency contact, substantiation, and removal at every decision point.
- **Funding-stream-driven service-mix.** Pre-FFPSA, federal financing favored removal over in-home services; FFPSA was designed to rebalance; implementation effects vary by jurisdiction.
- **Mandatory-reporter feedback loops** producing reporting concentration in particular institutional contexts (especially schools and hospitals) that may not reflect underlying maltreatment incidence.
- **Adversarial-system effects** — parents in juvenile-court cases often face systemic resource asymmetries against the Division and the GAL system.
- **Caseworker turnover and caseload-pressure effects** — high caseworker turnover, common in U.S. child welfare, produces continuity-of-decision-making failures and family-rapport failures.
- **Permanency-timeline pressure under ASFA** — the "twelve of twenty-two months" framework may produce permanency petitions on schedule-driven rather than family-readiness-driven timing.

**8.6 — Trade-secret / confidentiality declarations:**
- Category of restricted information: Children-services case records (ORC §5153.17 — perhaps the strongest non-HIPAA confidentiality regime in Ohio law); central-registry entries; HIPAA-protected health information of children in care; FERPA-protected educational information; juvenile-court records; mandatory-reporter identity protection.
- Source of restriction: Federal statute (HIPAA, FERPA, ASFA confidentiality requirements, ICWA tribal-notification provisions); Ohio Revised Code (§5153.17, §3109.412, Juv.R., ORC §149.43 exemptions).
- Auditability mitigation: Aggregate statistical disclosure; federal CFSR review; ODCY oversight; Ombudsman complaint review; juvenile-court proceedings (which are themselves partially closed but produce a record).
- R67 commitment: DCFS's authorized scope, the rules it operates under, federal CFSR aggregate outcomes, the broad pattern of its hotline-and-investigation volume, and its stop paths are visible from public sources. The individual-case detail that R67 would otherwise permit external review of is heavily restricted by federal and state law — restrictions that exist to protect children's privacy and that DCFS does not have authority to alter. R67 is met at the floor through aggregate disclosure and through the juvenile-court adversarial process, which provides individual-case review with full procedural protection.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**
ASSUMPTION: 60 months (approximately 1,800 days) for full wind-down. Justification: DCFS is a statutorily mandated county function under ORC §5153.04 (county commissioners' duty to designate a public children services agency); dismantling is not contemplated by statute except by designation of a successor under ORC §307.981. A wind-down would require (a) state and federal authorization for restructuring or successor designation; (b) transition planning for the ~14,000+ annual investigations and the children currently in custody (a count UNKNOWN from public sources reviewed; based on comparable urban-county data the figure is likely in the low-to-mid thousands); (c) records-management continuity for federally mandated AFCARS and SACWIS data; (d) successor-provider arrangements for every program area; (e) federal Title IV-E eligibility transfer or replacement; (f) personnel transition under civil-service and collective-bargaining rules; (g) ongoing-case continuity to ensure no child's safety or permanency is compromised by the transition. The 60-month estimate is conservative.

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**
- **Months 1–12:** Statutory authorization for restructuring; public notice as required by ORC §307.981(E); 60-day pre-designation notice to ODCY and ODJFS; successor identification (private agency, regional consortium, or restructured public entity); inventory of ongoing cases, federal eligibility, contracts, and records.
- **Months 12–24:** Transition planning for ongoing cases — each child in custody requires a permanency plan that survives the transition; each open investigation requires continuity; each foster home requires continued certification and payment.
- **Months 24–36:** Personnel transition; transition of federal Title IV-E and Title IV-B eligibility to successor entity; transition of contracts with PCPAs and residential providers.
- **Months 36–48:** Wind-down of legacy systems; transfer of physical records to successor; closure of ongoing cases or transfer to successor; ICPC outgoing-case continuity.
- **Months 48–60:** Final audit; federal compliance closeout; AFCARS and central-registry continuity verification; final litigation closeout.

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- **Federal Title IV-E eligibility.** Cannot be transferred to a new entity without federal sign-off; protocol is complex.
- **Permanent custody children and pending termination cases.** Cannot be paused; each child has a federally mandated permanency timeline.
- **Adoptions in process.** Cannot be paused without harm to children awaiting finalization.
- **Foster-home certifications.** Each foster caregiver has a federal/state-recognized status that must be transferred or recertified.
- **Open investigations.** Cannot be paused — federal CAPTA and state statute require timely investigation of accepted reports.
- **Central-registry continuity.** Statewide registry depends on county input.
- **Sibling separation risk** — placement decisions affecting siblings cannot be revisited during transition without harm.
- **Court orders in effect.** Hundreds-to-thousands of court orders in active cases continue until modified or terminated by the Juvenile Court.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**
- **Personal data (case records, central registry, AFCARS data):** Transferred to successor under federal-and-state confidentiality and continuity requirements; subject to applicable retention schedules; central-registry continuity must be verified with ODCY before any closing of legacy DCFS.
- **Institutional records (Board minutes, policies, contracts, fiscal records):** Archived to County under records-management schedules.
- **Derived outputs (substantiated findings, foster certifications, court orders, adoption finalizations):** Substantiated findings on central registry continue under the registry's permanent character; foster certifications transferred to successor; court orders survive any DCFS dissolution and continue to bind the successor; adoption finalizations are legally permanent and unaffected by agency dissolution.
- **Audit logs (hotline call recordings, case-system audit trails):** Retained per applicable schedules; preserved for pending litigation.

**9.5 — Downstream effect reversibility:**
DCFS outputs propagated to other systems include: ODCY central-registry entries (propagate to background-check vendors, employers in child-serving fields, out-of-state PCSAs, foster-licensing inquiries); AFCARS data (propagates to federal statistical systems); cross-reports to law enforcement (propagate to criminal-justice records); medical records of children in custody (propagate to healthcare systems); educational records (propagate to school systems). On dissolution of DCFS, none of these is automatically invalidated; reversal of individual entries requires individual administrative or judicial action; aggregate effects on systems are not reversed by agency dissolution.

**9.6 — Replacement feasibility:**
ORC §307.981 contemplates designation of private or government entities to perform family-services duties under specified procedures. Replacement could be (a) absorption into a state-administered system under direct ODCY administration; (b) designation of a private nonprofit PCSA-equivalent under §307.981; (c) regional consortium with adjacent-county PCSAs; (d) restructured public agency with different governance. ORC §307.981(H) provides specific procedural protections including a 60-day pre-designation notice requirement and a county-children-services-board opposition mechanism. Replacement is feasible but operationally and politically complex.

**9.7 — Reversibility verification:**
The declared 60-month timeline is estimated, not verified. ANNOTATION: DCFS has not, in publicly available materials reviewed, rehearsed dissolution. Per R43, the §9.1 declaration is treated as estimated.

**10.8 — Aggregate threshold justification:**
