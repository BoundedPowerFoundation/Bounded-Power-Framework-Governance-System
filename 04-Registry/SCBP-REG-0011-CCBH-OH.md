## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Cuyahoga County Board of Health (CCBH).

**1.2 — Functional description:**
CCBH is the governing board of a general health district under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 3709, operating since 1919. It is the local governmental public health authority for 58 communities in Cuyahoga County excluding the City of Cleveland (Cleveland operates its own city health district under separate authority). CCBH:
- Inspects and licenses food service operations and retail food establishments under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 3717 and Ohio Administrative Code Chapter 3717-1 (Food Code), conducting approximately 9,700 inspections annually across thousands of regulated establishments.
- Inspects and regulates swimming pools, spas, and public bathing places under ORC Chapter 3749 and OAC Chapter 3701-31.
- Permits, inspects, and enforces household sewage treatment systems (septic systems) under ORC Chapter 3718 and OAC Chapter 3701-29.
- Regulates plumbing in residential and certain non-residential structures within jurisdictions that have not retained their own plumbing-enforcement authority, under ORC §3781.06 and the CCBH Plumbing Regulation.
- Investigates and controls communicable disease under ORC Chapter 3701 and OAC Chapter 3701-3, with reportable-disease authority including investigation, contact tracing, isolation orders, and quarantine orders.
- Abates public-health nuisances under ORC §3707.01, including issuing orders compelling property owners to abate nuisances and prosecuting non-compliance.
- Regulates lead-hazard control activities (lead inspections, risk assessments, abatement) under ORC Chapter 3742.
- Inspects schools, child-care facilities, tattoo and body-piercing establishments, manufactured-home parks, and other regulated facilities.
- Issues certified copies of birth and death certificates as registrar of vital statistics within its district under ORC Chapter 3705.
- Investigates animal bites and rabies-exposure incidents under ORC Chapter 955 and ORC §3701.
- Operates nursing, clinical, maternal-and-child-health, school-health, HIV prevention, overdose-data, newborn-home-visiting, and child-fatality-review programs.
- Conducts beach water-quality monitoring and vector control.
- Issues administrative orders, levies fines under fee schedules, suspends and revokes operational licenses, and prosecutes through the Cuyahoga County Prosecutor.

**1.3 — Authority classification:**
[ ] Direct
[ ] Indirect
[x] Both — describe:
CCBH exercises **direct** authority through inspection-and-licensing decisions, abatement orders, isolation and quarantine orders, license revocation, and prosecution referrals. CCBH exercises **indirect** authority by acting as agent of the Ohio Department of Health (regulating multiple program areas under ODH delegation), by issuing public-health guidance documents that other authorities operationalize (e.g., the COVID-19 food-service guidance referenced by municipal officials in 2020), and by transmitting data and findings to other authorities (e.g., child-fatality findings to the Office of Early Childhood; communicable-disease data to the Ohio Department of Health and CDC).

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest actions CCBH can take against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Isolation order** (against a person known or suspected to have a communicable disease, restricting movement until non-infectious) and **quarantine order** (against persons exposed to a communicable disease, restricting movement during the disease's incubation period) under ORC §3701.13 and Chapter 3707. Enforcement is through the courts; violation is criminal.

- **Embargo or condemnation of adulterated or misbranded food** under ORC Chapter 3717.
- **Order to vacate or condemn an occupancy** as unfit for human habitation due to lead, sewage, or other public-health hazard.
- **Prosecution referral** to the Cuyahoga County Prosecutor for criminal violation of public-health law and CCBH regulations.
- **Civil penalties and administrative fines** under fee schedules adopted by Board resolution.
- **Mandatory reporting** of communicable disease, child abuse, animal bites, and lead exposure to specified state and federal authorities.

The Board does not exercise law-enforcement arrest authority directly; enforcement of orders runs through the courts and through cooperation with municipal police and the Sheriff.

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**
Public-health authority operates over bodily-harm subject matter in two distinct ways: (a) preventing harm through inspection, surveillance, and abatement; (b) imposing constraints on bodies through isolation and quarantine. Excess harm thresholds attach to both.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the rate of food-borne illness outbreaks traceable to CCBH-inspected establishments exceeds the rolling-three-year baseline by more than 50%, an internal-and-external structural review of the inspection program is initiated.

Threshold: If a CCBH isolation or quarantine order is shown on the record to have been issued without the procedural support required by ORC Chapter 3707 and the Fourteenth Amendment due-process minimum (notice; opportunity to be heard; written findings; appeal mechanism), the matter is reported under §11 and the order-issuance protocol is reviewed.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, more than 1.0% of issued isolation or quarantine orders are reversed or modified on judicial review for procedural deficiency, the procedural protocol is reviewed.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Judicial review.**
- Who can activate it: Any party adversely affected by a final CCBH decision — licensee, property owner, person subject to an isolation or quarantine order, person subject to a nuisance-abatement order. Review proceeds under ORC Chapter 2506 (appeals from administrative decisions of political subdivisions) or under direct civil action seeking injunctive relief in the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on the existence of a final administrative decision or an ongoing constitutional violation.
- What "stopped" means: A specific decision is reversed, modified, or remanded; an order is stayed or vacated; in structural-pattern cases, a court-ordered consent decree or injunction.
- Independence justification: The Court of Common Pleas is constitutionally separate from county government; judges are elected county-wide and not appointed by CCBH or the County Executive; the court is not funded from CCBH revenue.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Appellate review by the Ohio Eighth District Court of Appeals and (discretionarily) the Ohio Supreme Court. The 30-day appeal window under ORC §2506.01 governs initial filing.

**STOP PATH 2 — District Advisory Council action; Ohio Department of Health override.**
- Who can activate it: (a) The District Advisory Council — 22 mayors and trustees of the villages and townships of Cuyahoga County plus the Office of the Cuyahoga County Executive — which appoints members of the Board of Health and approves the District's annual appropriation; (b) the Ohio Department of Health, exercising supervisory authority under ORC Chapter 3709 and statewide-public-health authority under ORC Chapter 3701, including the power under ORC §3709.05 and §3709.03 to intervene in district affairs in specified circumstances.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based (DAC's annual budget cycle; ODH's accreditation and supervisory cycle) and judgment-based (a council member's introduction of a motion; ODH initiation of supervisory action).
- What "stopped" means: A class of CCBH activity is restructured or discontinued through DAC budget action; a specific Board member is replaced at term expiration or for cause; ODH may withdraw delegated authority for a specific program area (ORC §3717.45 et seq. license-program delegation; OAC Chapter 3701-29 sewage program delegation).
- Independence justification: The District Advisory Council is composed of elected officials of constituent municipalities — they are not CCBH employees and do not depend on CCBH for their own authority. ODH is structurally a state-level authority separate from Cuyahoga County.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: DAC actions occur on the public legislative timeline of constituent councils; ODH supervisory actions are subject to Ohio Administrative Procedure Act review.

**STOP PATH 3 — Ohio General Assembly statutory action.**
- Who can activate it: The Ohio General Assembly, by amendment of ORC Chapter 3709, 3707, 3701, or related chapters.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on legislative process; in practice, statewide-public-health controversies (such as those surfacing during the 2020–2022 pandemic period) have produced legislative attention to health-board authority statewide.
- What "stopped" means: Restructuring of general-health-district authority; modification of quarantine and isolation powers; adjustment of the budget mechanism; in the limit case, dissolution or reorganization of the general-health-district structure.
- Independence justification: The General Assembly is constitutionally separate from county and local government; it is not funded by CCBH and does not depend on CCBH for its authority.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ordinary legislative process under the Ohio Constitution.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 — Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):**

*Dependency A — Ohio Department of Health (ODH).* CCBH acts as ODH agent in regulating food safety, swimming pools and spas, household sewage treatment, lead-hazard control, communicable disease, and related program areas. ODH's rule-making operationalizes through CCBH inspection-and-enforcement. ANNOTATION: ODH is simultaneously a Type 1 dependency (its rules are operationalized through CCBH) and a Type 2 dependency (it is an independent state-level authority with supervisory power); the framework treats this dual character explicitly. Compensation structure: not a contractor relationship; ODH delegates by statute and rule.

*Dependency B — Contractors performing inspection, abatement, and environmental services* (Paragon CMS and similar firms appearing in CCBH agendas for environmental and lead-hazard work).
