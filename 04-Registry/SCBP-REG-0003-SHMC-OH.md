## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Shaker Heights Municipal Court (SHMC)

**1.2 — Functional description:**

The court is a single-judge court of limited jurisdiction established under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 1901. Within its five-municipality territory, the court adjudicates: misdemeanor criminal offenses (state and municipal); traffic offenses including OVI (operating a vehicle under the influence); civil cases up to $15,000; small claims; landlord-tenant matters including evictions; preliminary hearings in felony cases prior to transfer to the Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas; and civil traffic-camera enforcement cases referred from the participating municipalities. Operational outputs of the court include: monetary fines and court costs; orders of community service; orders of probation supervision including conditions; bench warrants for failure to appear; bonds and bond conditions; eviction judgments and writs of restitution; orders of protection in domestic violence matters; license suspensions and driving privileges orders referred to the Ohio Bureau of Motor Vehicles; and findings of liability or non-liability in civil and traffic-camera matters. The court operates a Domestic Violence Docket (established February 2022) that applies enhanced supervision and resource referrals to qualifying cases. The court refers offenders to two specialty dockets operated outside the court: the Mental Health Court and a Treatment Docket. Cases handled in 2024 included those generated by the five police departments in the court's territory and by civil and small-claims filings from the public.

**1.3 — Authority classification:**
- [X] Direct
- [ ] Indirect — describe the form of indirect influence:
- [ ] Both

The court issues binding directives — judgments, orders, sentences, warrants, and writs — within its statutory jurisdiction. ANNOTATION: An argument could be made for "both," in that the court's outputs (criminal records, eviction records, license-suspension referrals) are operationalized downstream by background-check vendors, landlords, employers, the Ohio BMV, and consumer-reporting systems in ways the court does not control. The bundle's R58 framing would treat this as indirect-authority territory worth declaring. The third-party draft conservatively records "direct" with this note; a self-filing by the court should consider whether §3.5 indirect-influence boundaries should be populated.

Ohio Revised Code Chapter 1901 (Municipal Court statute), specifically:

**2.2 — Approximate number of people directly affected:**

ANNOTATION: This requires two figures. Resident population of the five-municipality territory is approximately 63,600 (2020 Census: Shaker Heights ~29,000; Beachwood ~14,040; University Heights ~13,000; Pepper Pike ~6,796; Hunting Valley ~763). The actually-affected population is substantially larger because the territory includes major commuter corridors (Lee Road, Chagrin Boulevard, I-271 access, Cedar Road, Warrensville Center Road) and commercial centers (Beachwood Place, Eton Chagrin) drawing non-resident traffic and visitors who may be subject to the court for offenses committed within the territory. Estimate of total affected population in any given year (residents + non-resident drivers + commercial visitors + parties to civil filings): on the order of 150,000–250,000 distinct persons annually. The estimate here uses 150,000 as a conservative figure. The court's 2024 annual report shows the court collected approximately $2.78 million in fines, costs, and state fees on a 2024 caseload consistent with the 10,000–12,000 cases-per-year historical range. Conservative estimate of distinct defendants/parties touched by the court annually: 8,000–12,000.

**2.3 — Of the affected population, what number/proportion are non-consenting:**

Essentially all of it. Court jurisdiction is statutory and does not depend on the consent of the persons subject to it. Residents of the five municipalities do not opt in to the court; they are governed by it because they live there. Drivers passing through do not opt in; they are governed by it because they drive there. Defendants in criminal, traffic, and civil cases do not opt in; they are summoned. The estimate of 150,000 in §2.2 is used as the non-consenting affected population for §10.7 threshold calibration.

The voluntary-but-stuck consideration in the form note applies in a structural sense: residents could in principle leave the five municipalities, but the cost of relocation (housing equity, employment, schools, family ties, mortgage transaction costs) is substantial. For purposes of this record, the resident population is treated as non-consenting.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**

Statutory ceilings under Ohio law:
- Maximum sentence: 180 days in jail for a first-degree misdemeanor (Ohio R.C. §2929.24); 90 days for second-degree; 60 days for third-degree; 30 days for fourth-degree
- Maximum fine: $1,000 plus court costs for a first-degree misdemeanor (Ohio R.C. §2929.28); lower for lesser degrees

- Eviction: writ of restitution following judgment in forcible entry and detainer
- Bond: discretionary, subject to Ohio Crim. R. 46
- Bench warrants: for failure to appear or contempt

**3.5 — Indirect influence boundaries:**

ANNOTATION: Per §1.3 the court is recorded as direct. However, the court's outputs are operationalized indirectly by several downstream systems in ways that materially shape decisions affecting the persons before it:
- Criminal-record information from SHMC adjudications enters Ohio BCI databases and downstream commercial background-check products. These products materially shape employment screening, tenant screening, professional licensing, and credit/insurance underwriting. The affected party experiences these downstream effects but the underlying decisions are made by third parties.
- Eviction-judgment records from SHMC enter tenant-screening databases (CoreLogic, RealPage, TransUnion SmartMove, and similar) and materially shape future rental-housing access. The eviction-record persistence is typically 7 years on consumer reports under FCRA §605.
- Conviction and license-suspension records referred to the Ohio BMV materially shape driving privileges, vehicle insurance pricing, and CDL eligibility.

The court does not control how its outputs are operationalized by these downstream systems. R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition) and R60 (Derived Authority Inheritance) suggest these downstream chains are within the framework's structural scope. A self-filing would commit to specific indirect-influence boundaries here.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

> ANNOTATION on harm thresholds: The court has not declared structural harm thresholds in the framework's sense. The thresholds below are reconstructed from publicly available peer-benchmark data for Ohio municipal courts and from rates of court-attributable harm tracked by external bodies (Ohio Supreme Court Office of Court Services, Disciplinary Counsel, Department of Justice settlements with peer courts). A self-filing would substitute the court's own committed thresholds.

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**

Threshold (reconstructed peer-benchmark): If any in-custody death, serious bodily injury, or use-of-force incident occurs in the court's bailiff-operated holding cells, prisoner-transport, or courtroom-security operations in any calendar year exceeding zero, an independent review is initiated. The court itself does not operate detention facilities (defendants in custody are held at city or county jails), but the court's order is the cause of confinement. Peer benchmark: most Ohio municipal courts report zero in-custody incidents annually.

ANNOTATION: A self-filing would also need to address rates of court-attributable bodily harm via bond decisions (e.g., domestic-violence cases where bond release precedes a homicide of the protected party). Ohio has tracked this category since the late 2010s. Peer benchmark thresholds would be expressed in incidents-per-thousand-DV-cases.

**4.2 — Agency impairment threshold:**

Threshold: If the rate of evictions resulting in homelessness within the court's territory exceeds the rate observed in comparably-sized eastern-Cuyahoga peer courts by more than 50% in any rolling 12-month period, a review is initiated. If the rate of criminal cases proceeding without counsel for indigent defendants entitled to counsel exceeds the rate set in Ohio Supreme Court Office of Court Services peer-comparable data by more than 20% in any rolling 12-month period, a review is initiated. If the rate of bond decisions resulting in pretrial detention solely due to inability to pay (rather than flight risk or danger to community) exceeds rates established by Ohio's bail reform monitoring framework, a review is initiated.

ANNOTATION: Specific numerical thresholds would require access to the court's case-level data, which is partially available through Ohio Supreme Court Office of Court Services reports.

**4.3 — Ecological damage threshold:**
- [X] Not applicable — justification: The court's operations do not generate material ecological output beyond ordinary office-building utilities consumption at 3355 Lee Road. The court does not adjudicate environmental matters (this is the Franklin County Municipal Court's environmental division, not SHMC) and has no environmental-enforcement authority.

**4.4 — Generational binding threshold:**

Threshold: If the proportion of court-ordered fines, costs, or restitution obligations that remain unpaid and active against defendants for more than 10 years exceeds the rate observed in peer Ohio municipal courts, a review is initiated. Court-ordered debt persists; criminal records persist (with sealing/expungement available under Ohio R.C. §2953 series); eviction records persist on consumer reports for 7 years under FCRA. Court findings can bind the defendant's housing access, employment access, and credit access for years to decades.

ANNOTATION: Ohio's 2023 expansion of record-sealing eligibility (S.B. 288) has reduced some of this persistence. A self-filing would specify thresholds in measurable form.

**4.5 — Communicative suppression threshold:**

Threshold: If court records (case files, dockets, orders, judgments not under seal) are unavailable to the public for more than 30 days in any rolling 12-month period due to technology failure, policy decision, or sealing-rule misapplication, a review is initiated. The court has committed (per 2022 Annual Report) to public-document access including images of publicly available documents, funded by a 2022 Ohio Supreme Court technology grant. If contempt orders or gag orders are imposed in ways that materially restrict public reporting on the court's operations, a review is initiated.

**4.6 — Informational sovereignty threshold:**

Threshold: Court records persist indefinitely as a matter of Ohio Rules of Superintendence on record retention. Sealing and expungement are available under Ohio R.C. §2953 series. ANNOTATION: The threshold here is structural — the framework's R22 (Informational Sovereignty Constraint) and SCBP-09 §VI suggest that indefinite informational indexing of individuals is itself a harm category requiring measurable thresholds. The court would need to declare a threshold tying record-persistence policy to the population most affected (defendants whose cases ended in dismissal or acquittal, defendants whose cases are eligible for sealing but have not been sealed because the defendant cannot navigate the petition process pro se).

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

> ANNOTATION on stop paths: For a state-statute-created court, the stop paths are unusual relative to administrative authorities. The court cannot be "dismantled" in the same way a parking enforcement bureau can — it exists by statute. The stop paths therefore distinguish between (a) stopping a particular action of the court (appeal, mandamus, recusal), (b) stopping the judge's authority (disciplinary action, removal, election defeat), and (c) stopping the court's existence as a unit (statutory amendment by the Ohio General Assembly).

**STOP PATH 1: Appellate review (Eighth District Court of Appeals)**

- Who can activate it: Any party adversely affected by a final order, judgment, or sentence of the court may file a notice of appeal under Ohio App. R. 4 within 30 days. The Cuyahoga County Prosecutor or municipal prosecutors may appeal certain pretrial rulings under Ohio R.C. §2945.67.
- Trigger condition: Final order of the court adverse to the appellant. Threshold-based (the existence of a final order) combined with judgment-based (appellant's choice to appeal).
- What "stopped" means: The appellate court may affirm, reverse, modify, or remand. A stay pending appeal may be granted, halting enforcement of the order. On reversal, the order is vacated.
- Independence justification: The Eighth District Court of Appeals is a state appellate court structurally independent of SHMC. Its judges are elected statewide-by-district and have no reporting relationship to SHMC. It is funded by the State of Ohio, not by the five municipalities.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Standard appellate timeline; merits decisions typically issued within 6–18 months of full briefing. Stays pending appeal may be granted within days under Ohio App. R. 7.

**STOP PATH 2: Judicial disciplinary action and removal**

- Who can activate it: Any person may file a grievance with the Ohio Office of Disciplinary Counsel or the Cuyahoga County Bar Association's Certified Grievance Committee against the judge. The Ohio Board of Professional Conduct adjudicates discipline. The Supreme Court of Ohio imposes final discipline up to and including suspension or disbarment. Removal from office is governed by Ohio Constitution Art. IV §17 (impeachment by the General Assembly) and ORC §2701.11 (concurrent resolution removal) and the Supreme Court's removal jurisdiction.
- Trigger condition: Violation of the Ohio Code of Judicial Conduct, conviction of a felony or misdemeanor of moral turpitude, or other defined cause.
- What "stopped" means: Suspension, disbarment, or removal of the judge. If the judge is suspended or removed, the court continues to operate under an acting or assigned judge per ORC §1901.121.
- Independence justification: Disciplinary Counsel and the Board of Professional Conduct are state-level entities outside the court. The Supreme Court of Ohio sits above all Ohio courts.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Grievance investigation may take 6–24 months; formal disciplinary proceedings add 12+ months. Interim suspension is available in serious cases.

**STOP PATH 3: Electoral defeat and statutory amendment**

- Who can activate it: Voters of the five-municipality territory at six-year intervals (nonpartisan judicial election under Ohio R.C. §1901.07). Separately, the Ohio General Assembly may amend ORC Chapter 1901 to alter the court's jurisdiction, eliminate the court, or consolidate it with another court.
- Trigger condition: Electoral threshold (majority vote of those casting ballots in the judicial race). Statutory amendment threshold (passage by both houses of the General Assembly and governor's signature, or override).
- What "stopped" means: A new judge takes office; or the court's jurisdiction is reduced or eliminated; or the court is consolidated into another municipal court.
- Independence justification: The electorate is structurally independent. The General Assembly is structurally independent.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Elections occur on a fixed schedule. The current judge took office January 2022, six-year term ending December 2027. Statutory amendment is on the General Assembly's own timeline.

**5.4 — Penalty for activating stop:**
- [X] Confirmed — no penalty applies. Appellants pay filing fees; disciplinary complainants are protected by Ohio Rule for the Government of the Judiciary; voters cannot be retaliated against for their vote. ANNOTATION: As a practical matter, persons engaged with the court on an ongoing basis (defendants on probation, parties in pending eviction matters) may face informal pressures that constrain their willingness to challenge the court while their cases are active. This is a structural concern across all courts, not specific to SHMC.
- [ ] Exception claimed

Second, mandamus or prohibition writs from a higher court can compel or restrain action by SHMC; these are appellate-court remedies and are encompassed within Stop Path 1.

## SECTION 6 — What does it depend on?

**6.1 — Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):**

1. **Magistrates** (1 full-time, 2 part-time traffic magistrates). Function: hearing assigned dockets under the judge's authority, with judgments subject to the judge's adoption. Contractual relationship: court employees appointed by the judge under ORC §1901.32. Compensation structure: [X] Flat fee per action — they are salaried; not revenue-share or outcome-tied.

2. **Probation Department** (court probation officers). Function: monitoring probationers, drug testing, referrals to treatment, violation reporting. Contractual relationship: court employees. Compensation: salaried.

ANNOTATION: Note that bailiffs, clerks of court, and other court staff are operational employees but are not "acting under" the court's adjudicative authority in the same sense; they are recorded under §3.3 resource ceiling. The Type 1 distinction here is for decision-makers and quasi-adjudicators.

**6.2 — Type 2 dependencies (independent authorities with structural relationship):**

1. **Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas** — receives felony cases on transfer from SHMC after preliminary hearing; receives appeals of certain civil matters. Downstream: SHMC bind felonies over to Common Pleas. Upstream: Common Pleas does not direct SHMC's docket but its administrative rules may affect Cuyahoga County practices generally.
2. **Eighth District Court of Appeals** — receives appeals from SHMC. Upstream relationship.
3. **Supreme Court of Ohio** — promulgates Rules of Superintendence and Rules of Practice that govern SHMC; provides technology grants and administrative oversight. Upstream relationship.
4. **City of Shaker Heights** (home municipality) — funds court operations (mandatory under ORC §1901.11 and §1901.36), maintains the courthouse facility at 3355 Lee Road, distributes collected fines per statute. Mixed upstream/downstream relationship.
5. **The other four participating municipalities** (Beachwood, University Heights, Pepper Pike, Hunting Valley) — share court expenses and receive distributions of fines per ORC §1901.11. Downstream relationship.
6. **Five municipal police departments** (Shaker Heights, Beachwood, University Heights, Pepper Pike, Hunting Valley) — file the bulk of criminal and traffic cases that constitute the court's docket. Upstream relationship: police case filings drive court caseload.
7. **City prosecutors of the five municipalities and the Cuyahoga County Prosecutor's Office** — prosecute cases before SHMC. Upstream relationship.

ANNOTATION: Other relevant Type 2 dependencies include the Ohio Bureau of Motor Vehicles (operationalizes license-suspension referrals), Cuyahoga County Sheriff's Department (warrant service and transport), Cuyahoga County Jail and city jails (custody during sentence), Cuyahoga County Public Defender's Office (counsel for indigent defendants), the Mental Health Court and Treatment Docket operated by Common Pleas (specialty referrals), and the Cuyahoga County Domestic Violence Task Force.

**6.3 — Type 3 dependencies (infrastructure whose failure would compromise stop paths or auditability):**

1. **Court case-management system** — if the case management system fails or is corrupted, dockets, public records, and audit capability are compromised. The 2022 Ohio Supreme Court technology grant funded an update; vendor identity not specified in public records.
2. **Public-records access infrastructure** (court website at shakerheightscourt.org and online case search) — if records access is lost, R67 minimum auditability and R81 adversarial exposure are compromised. The court has committed to mobile-accessible online docket per 2022 Annual Report.

**6.4 — Capture risk identification:**

- [ ] No
- [X] Yes — identify and describe the structural risk:

Two structural capture risks identified:

(a) **Revenue-dependency alignment.** The court collects approximately $2.78 million annually in fines, costs, and state fees against a budget of approximately $2.46 million. While statutory distribution sends much of this revenue to entities other than the court itself (state, county, the five municipalities per ORC §1901.31), the participating municipalities have a financial interest in case-filing volume because fines are distributed to them. Police departments — the upstream Type 2 dependency — are operated by the same municipalities. This is a recognized structural pattern in U.S. municipal courts (see U.S. Department of Justice Ferguson report 2015; subsequent federal litigation in multiple jurisdictions) and is the canonical R03/R66 capture-risk scenario for municipal courts. The 2024 Annual Report's framing ("It is noteworthy that without case filings there can be no moneys to collect") makes the structural relationship explicit. No public evidence indicates SHMC has been the subject of federal investigation or settlement on this issue, but the structural risk exists and is declarable.

(b) **Single-judge structural concentration.** SHMC is a single-judge court. The judge controls magistrate appointments, probation department, bailiff department, and the court's local-rules-making power. While this concentration is statutory (ORC §1901.08 sets the number of judges for SHMC), it concentrates authority in a way that depends on the judge's individual judgment, ethical compliance, and operational decisions. The R03 capture-risk surface is reduced by the elected nature of the position and by the disciplinary system, but is not zero. Historical public-record allegations against the prior judge (covering 2010s–2021) are an example of how single-judge concentration creates oversight challenges.

**6.5 — Reversibility-affecting dependencies:**

The court cannot be "dismantled" in the framework's usual sense; it exists by statute. The reversibility question that applies to SHMC is reversibility of its outputs. Dependencies that affect output reversibility:

1. **Ohio BMV** — license suspensions referred by SHMC are operationalized by BMV. Reversal on appeal requires BMV processing time after the appellate ruling.
2. **Background-check vendors and consumer reporting agencies** — criminal-record and eviction-record outputs propagate to commercial databases. Reversal upon sealing or expungement under Ohio R.C. §2953 requires the affected person to navigate the petition process and may not fully purge from all downstream consumer reports (FCRA-governed). R65 propagation duties apply.
3. **The five municipal police departments and prosecutors' offices** — case files, arrest records, and prosecutorial records may persist independent of court-record sealing.

**6.6 — Coordination disclosures:**

The five municipalities, their five police departments, and SHMC together function as a coordinated criminal-justice and ordinance-enforcement system for the territory. Under R55 (Fragmentation Must Not Defeat Containment), this system can be evaluated as a single authority system for framework purposes even though it is legally composed of seven distinct entities (court + 5 municipalities + Cuyahoga County to the degree the County overlays). ANNOTATION: This is a finding worth noting; it is not an accusation. Multi-municipality municipal courts are a deliberate Ohio statutory structure for cost-sharing among smaller jurisdictions.

**6.8 — External authority operationalization (R72):**

The court operationalizes external decisions in several ways:

1. **External authority: Ohio General Assembly (substantive law).** The court applies Ohio R.C. provisions (traffic law, criminal law, landlord-tenant law) created by the legislature. The affected party can distinguish "the court decided" from "the statute prescribes" only to a limited degree; the practical experience for many defendants is "the court convicted me."
2. **External authority: The five municipalities (ordinances).** SHMC enforces the five municipalities' codes (parking, noise, animal control, code violations, traffic-camera ordinances). Whether the court is enforcing state law or municipal ordinance is often legally significant but operationally indistinguishable to the defendant.
3. **External authority: Police departments (charging decisions).** The case before SHMC reflects the charging decision made by the five police departments and their prosecutors. The court adjudicates within the charges presented; charging discretion lies outside the court.
4. **External authority: Prosecutorial offices (plea decisions, dismissals, charge reductions).** Plea agreements, charge amendments, and prosecutorial dismissals operate outside the court's initiation but are operationalized through the court's docket.
5. **External authority: Traffic-camera vendors and the contracting municipalities (camera-based liability determinations).** Per the court's website, camera-violation cases are now under municipal court jurisdiction (no longer Mayor's Court) following Ohio Supreme Court rulings. The factual record originates with the camera-vendor system; the court reviews and adjudicates contests.

ANNOTATION: R72 (External System Equivalence Rule) suggests these operationalized external decisions are within SHMC's authority surface for compliance purposes. A self-filing would need to address each.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

> ANNOTATION: As a court created by statute, SHMC does not "end" in the way an administrative authority does. The framework's R28 expiration default applies awkwardly. The relevant questions are: (a) how does a particular judicial term end; (b) how could the court be eliminated or consolidated; and (c) how are its outputs reversed when wrong.

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**

Three different timelines apply:
- Judicial term: 6 years (next election 2027).
- Statutory elimination: subject to Ohio General Assembly action. Historical precedent suggests 1–2 years from legislative initiation to effective date.
- Functional consolidation: merger with another Cuyahoga County municipal court would require ORC amendment and a transitional plan; estimate 12–24 months.

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**

If consolidation or elimination were undertaken:
- Statutory amendment by Ohio General Assembly (months 1–12)
- Transition planning: case-transfer to receiving court, records migration, staff transition, facility disposition (months 6–18)
- Effective date of consolidation (month 12–24)
- Wind-down of residual obligations (probation supervision, pending appeals, outstanding warrants) at receiving court (ongoing)

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**

Significant entrenchment factors:
- Statutory permanence — SHMC exists by ORC; political effort required to amend.
- Fiscal dependency of the five municipalities on the court's revenue distribution.
- Local political constituency (judicial elections, municipal council relationships).
- Real estate at 3355 Lee Road owned/maintained by the City of Shaker Heights for court use.
- Staff employment continuity.
- Pending caseload at any given moment (typically 10,000+ active cases annually).

These factors mean SHMC is structurally difficult to eliminate quickly. The framework's R61 (Anti-Entrenchment Constraint) is in tension with the statutory permanence of state courts; this is a finding worth declaring rather than dismissing.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**

If the court were dissolved or consolidated:
- Personal data: case records retained per Ohio Rules of Superintendence record-retention schedules (varies by case type, generally 5–25 years).
- Institutional records: retained per Ohio Public Records Act and Rules of Superintendence.
- Derived outputs: criminal records, eviction records, license suspensions already propagated to downstream systems (Ohio BCI, consumer reporting, BMV) would persist independently of SHMC's continued existence.
- Audit logs: retained per applicable Ohio Rules.

ANNOTATION: R63 (Downstream Effect Reversibility) is structurally challenging for criminal-court outputs because propagation has already occurred and downstream systems (consumer reporting, BMV, federal databases) operate under independent legal authorities.
