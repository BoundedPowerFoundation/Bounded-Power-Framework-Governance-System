## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** The Cleveland Electric Illuminating Company (legal name; abbreviated CEI), operating publicly under the trade name **The Illuminating Company**. CEI is a wholly-owned subsidiary of FirstEnergy Corp. (NYSE: FE), a publicly traded investor-owned utility holding company headquartered in Akron, Ohio. CEI is one of three FirstEnergy regulated electric distribution utilities in Ohio (the others being Ohio Edison Company and The Toledo Edison Company).

**1.2 — Functional description:**
CEI is a regulated electric distribution utility under Ohio law, certified by the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio (PUCO) to provide electric distribution service within its defined service territory. Following Ohio's electric-industry deregulation under Senate Bill 3 (1999), CEI's authorized function is **distribution only** — generation is provided by competitive suppliers selected by customers or by CEI as default-service provider under the Standard Service Offer / Price to Compare. CEI functions include:
- **Operation and maintenance of the electric distribution system** including substations, distribution lines, transformers, meters, and service connections from the high-voltage transmission grid down to individual customer premises.
- **Service connection** for new customers, including residential, commercial, and industrial connections.
- **Disconnection of service** for non-payment, safety issues, customer-requested termination, or other circumstances authorized by Ohio Administrative Code Chapter 4901:1-10 (PUCO rules for electric service and safety standards) and Chapter 4901:1-18 (termination of residential service).
- **Metering** of electricity consumption and billing for distribution charges; consolidated billing on behalf of customer-selected generation suppliers under PUCO rules.
- **Default-service generation provision** at the Price to Compare (PTC) for customers who do not select a competitive supplier (residential PTC approximately 9.5¢/kWh as of May 2026).
- **Outage response and restoration** following routine equipment failures and weather events.
- **Storm-restoration operations** including pre-deployment coordination with mutual-aid partners and post-event restoration.
- **Grid modernization and infrastructure investment** under PUCO-approved capital programs, including the Grid Mod I (completed) and Grid Mod II (proposed) plans.
- **Public-safety coordination** with first responders for downed lines, electrical fires, and emergency response.
- **Demand-response and load-management programs** under PUCO-approved tariffs.
- **Customer-assistance programs** including the Percentage of Income Payment Plan (PIPP Plus) administered through the Ohio Department of Development.
- **Tariff administration** of all rates, charges, and conditions of service approved by PUCO.
- **Reporting** to PUCO on reliability, outage statistics, financial performance, corporate-separation compliance, and other matters required by Ohio Administrative Code.

[ ] Direct

[x] Both — describe:
CEI exercises **direct** authority through every action affecting an individual customer's electric service: service connection or disconnection, meter reading and billing, outage response prioritization, restoration sequencing, and the day-to-day operational decisions that determine whether power is available at a customer premise. The disconnect-for-non-payment authority — within PUCO procedural rules — is the most consequential single direct-authority action. CEI exercises **indirect** authority through (a) the rate-case and tariff-filing process before PUCO, where CEI's proposed rates and terms shape the regulatory record on which PUCO decisions rest; (b) the infrastructure-investment-and-deferral choices that determine multi-decade reliability trajectories for specific communities; (c) the framing of customer-choice and Price-to-Compare communications that materially shape household and business decisions about competitive suppliers; (d) participation in defining utility-industry standards through trade-association activity and grid-modernization technology selection. Under R58 (Indirect Authority Recognition), the rate-case input, infrastructure-deferral pattern, and customer-communication framing are themselves authority for compliance purposes.

## SECTION 3 — What can it do, and what can it not do?

**3.1 — Geographic reach:**
[x] Multi-county — five counties in Northeast Ohio: Ashtabula, Cuyahoga, Geauga, Lake, and Lorain. CEI's certified service territory has fixed geographic boundaries within these counties. CEI does not exercise distribution authority in areas served by Cleveland Public Power (a city-owned municipal utility serving approximately 75,000 customers in portions of the City of Cleveland) or Painesville Municipal Electric, or in areas served by adjacent FirstEnergy subsidiaries Ohio Edison and Toledo Edison. CEI does not exercise authority outside Ohio.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest actions CEI can take against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Termination (disconnection) of electric service** for non-payment, safety issues, customer-requested termination, theft of service, or other authorized circumstances. Disconnection is procedurally bounded by OAC Chapter 4901:1-18 (notice requirements, medical-certification protections, winter-period restrictions, payment-plan availability). For households with medically certified residents, disconnection is restricted; for low-income households, PIPP Plus provides an alternative payment structure.
- **Refusal to connect new service** at premises with unresolved prior-account balances, safety issues, or unmet conditions of service (subject to PUCO rules and customer-protection provisions).
- **Security deposit requirements** for customers without acceptable credit or with prior delinquency history (bounded by PUCO rules).
- **Rate increases through PUCO rate-case process** affecting all customers in the service territory; the May 2024 rate-case application sought $190.3 million in additional annual revenue across the three FirstEnergy Ohio utilities, with the November 2025 PUCO order granting approximately $76 million in additional annual revenue specifically for CEI's approximately 745,000 customers (subject to subsequent affordability-driven revision in February 2026).
- **Service-territory protection** — under Ohio law, customers within CEI's certified service territory cannot take distribution service from another utility (regulated-monopoly structure).
- **Tariff enforcement** — collection of all rates, riders, and charges set forth in PUCO-approved tariffs, including late-payment charges, reconnection fees, security deposits, and various standard charges.
- **Easement enforcement and vegetation management** — CEI exercises easement rights on private property for distribution-line maintenance, including tree-trimming and removal where the trees pose a reliability or safety risk.
- **Outage-response prioritization** — sequencing of restoration during multi-customer outages, which has practical effects on which customers regain service first and is influenced by feeder-circuit configuration and grid-modernization investment patterns.
- **Construction in public rights-of-way** under franchise agreements with municipalities.
- **Customer-data sharing** with competitive suppliers under PUCO-approved data-sharing programs for customer-choice operation.
- **Civil collection action** including referral to collection agencies and credit-bureau reporting for unpaid balances.

CEI does not exercise law-enforcement arrest authority. CEI cannot, by its own action, set rates outside the PUCO-approved tariff. CEI cannot deny generation-supplier competition under Ohio's deregulated framework. CEI cannot expand its service territory outside the PUCO-certified boundaries without PUCO authorization. CEI cannot terminate service in violation of the procedural protections in OAC Chapter 4901:1-18.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**
Distribution-utility operations carry several recognized bodily-harm risk categories: electrical contact with energized lines; electrical fires from infrastructure failure; medical-equipment outage effects on medically dependent customers; heat-and-cold extremes when service is interrupted during extreme weather; carbon-monoxide and fire risks from emergency-heating use during outages.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the rate of customer-reported medically-dependent-customer outage events exceeds peer-utility benchmarks by more than 25%, the medical-customer-prioritization protocol is reviewed and the pattern is reported under §11.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the System Average Interruption Duration Index (SAIDI), System Average Interruption Frequency Index (SAIFI), or Customer Average Interruption Duration Index (CAIDI) for any defined sub-territory (e.g., a city or feeder-circuit group) exceeds the system-wide average by more than 50%, the sub-territory pattern is disclosed under §11 and the underlying infrastructure investment posture is reviewed. ANNOTATION: The 2025 PUCO notice-of-probable-non-compliance regarding the Lakewood area indicates that such sub-territory disparity has historically existed without triggering a structural-disclosure obligation; this threshold formalizes it.

Threshold: If a fatality or serious injury occurs that is attributable to CEI infrastructure (downed lines, equipment failure, vegetation contact with energized lines, transformer fire), the event is reported under PUCO rules and under §11 within 30 days, with root-cause analysis disclosed within 180 days.

Threshold: If an outage during an extreme-weather event exceeds 96 hours duration for any individual customer, the case is documented for review of (a) whether outage duration was driven by infrastructure deferral patterns, (b) whether storm-restoration prioritization adequately considered medically-dependent customers, and (c) whether mutual-aid resource deployment was timely.

Threshold: If line-worker fatality or serious-injury rates exceed Edison Electric Institute peer benchmarks by more than 25% in any rolling 24-month period, the underlying work-practice protocols are reviewed.

**4.7 — Additional harm thresholds:**

Category name: **Infrastructure-investment-deferral pattern concentration.**

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Public Utilities Commission of Ohio (PUCO) regulatory authority and direct appellate review.**
- Who can activate it: (a) PUCO Commissioners and staff exercising rate-case, tariff-review, complaint-resolution, and reliability-oversight authority under ORC Title 49; (b) any person or entity affected by a CEI tariff or practice, through PUCO complaint procedures; (c) the Office of the Ohio Consumers' Counsel (OCC), the statutorily independent state advocate for utility consumers, currently directed by Maureen Willis, with authority under ORC §4911 to intervene in PUCO proceedings and represent residential consumer interests; (d) parties to PUCO proceedings, through direct appeal to the Ohio Supreme Court under ORC §4903.13 (Ohio is one of the few states in which utility-rate appeals proceed directly from the regulator to the Supreme Court, bypassing intermediate appellate courts).
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based (scheduled rate cases; reliability reviews; annual reports) and complaint-driven.
- What "stopped" means: Rate-case applications denied or partially granted (the November 2025 order granting only $76 million of FirstEnergy's $190.3 million requested increase, and the February 2026 order reducing storm-restoration cost recovery, are direct examples); tariff terms modified or rejected; corrective-action orders imposed (the July 2025 notice-of-probable-non-compliance regarding Lakewood is an example); civil monetary penalties imposed (the HB 6 enforcement resulted in $250.7 million in restitution and forfeitures, with a subsequent settlement adding to that total); in extreme circumstances, certification suspension or revocation.
- Independence justification: PUCO Commissioners are appointed by the Governor with Senate confirmation and serve five-year staggered terms; the OCC is statutorily independent of PUCO and of utilities; the Ohio Supreme Court is constitutionally separate. ANNOTATION: The HB 6 scandal demonstrated that PUCO independence is not absolute — former Chair Sam Randazzo was charged federally in connection with FirstEnergy payments. The framework concern is structural; PUCO's institutional independence remains a meaningful stop path despite the documented historical capture event.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: PUCO administrative process under ORC Chapter 4903 (rehearing and appeal); direct appeal to the Ohio Supreme Court under ORC §4903.13 within 60 days of PUCO's final order.

**STOP PATH 2 — Federal regulatory authority; criminal enforcement; private litigation.**
- Who can activate it: The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) on transmission and bulk-power-system matters; the U.S. Department of Justice for criminal prosecution (which obtained the $230 million federal fine against FirstEnergy in 2021 and the convictions of Larry Householder, Matt Borges, and others in the HB 6 case); the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission for securities-fraud and disclosure-related conduct; private plaintiffs through class-action litigation (multiple HB 6-related class actions have been filed); ratepayer plaintiffs in state-court actions where PUCO jurisdiction is not exclusive; the Ohio Attorney General in consumer-protection and antitrust enforcement.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on federal enforcement priorities; complaint-driven for litigation.
- What "stopped" means: Federal criminal convictions and fines; SEC enforcement actions and civil monetary penalties; class-action judgments and settlements; consent decrees imposing structural reform; in the limit case, criminal charging of corporate executives.
- Independence justification: Federal agencies and courts are structurally separate from CEI and from state-level Ohio authority. The U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Ohio adjudicated the HB 6 criminal cases.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Federal Administrative Procedure Act for agency action; ordinary criminal and civil litigation procedure.

**STOP PATH 3 — Legislative action; municipal franchise authority; market-and-reputational discipline.**
- Who can activate it: The Ohio General Assembly, through amendment of ORC Title 49 and the deregulation framework — including specific HB 6 partial-repeal legislation; municipal governments within CEI's service territory, through franchise-agreement renegotiation and through municipal-aggregation programs (Cleveland CCA, Cuyahoga County aggregation programs) that affect customer-choice patterns; the U.S. Congress, through amendment of the Federal Power Act and related statutes; capital-market discipline, through bond-rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) that affect CEI's parent company's cost of capital; customer-and-civic reputational dynamics; the Cleveland Browns' termination of the FirstEnergy Stadium naming-rights agreement in 2023 — a reputational consequence of the HB 6 scandal demonstrating that market-and-reputational discipline operates.
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based on legislative process and capital-market cycles; episodic on reputational events.
- What "stopped" means: Statutory restructuring of the regulatory framework (the partial repeal of HB 6 provisions is an example); municipal-aggregation programs that shift customer-choice patterns away from utility-default service; bond-rating downgrades increasing cost of capital; reputational consequences including loss of brand-affiliation arrangements.
- Independence justification: The Ohio General Assembly is constitutionally separate from CEI; municipal governments are independent local authorities; capital markets and reputational dynamics operate independently of CEI.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ordinary legislative process; market processes operate continuously.

**6.1 — Type 1 dependencies (operational extensions acting under this authority):**

*Dependency A — FirstEnergy Corp. corporate parent and shared services.* Function: corporate strategy, financial management, capital allocation, regulatory affairs, human resources, information technology, and other shared services across the three FirstEnergy Ohio utilities (CEI, Ohio Edison, Toledo Edison) and the broader FirstEnergy ten-utility system. *Dependency B — Contracted line-construction, vegetation-management, and substation-maintenance vendors.* Function: a substantial portion of CEI's field work is performed by contracted crews, particularly during storm-restoration events. Major contractors include national line-construction firms whose specific identification is in PUCO filings and CEI vendor records. Compensation structure: per-project contracts; storm-event surge contracts; long-term maintenance contracts.

*Dependency C — Mutual-aid utility partners.* Function: during major storm events, CEI receives crews from utilities across the U.S. through industry mutual-aid arrangements coordinated by the Edison Electric Institute and regional organizations. [x] Yes — identify and describe the structural risk:

Six structural risks are visible from public sources, several with documented historical realization:

**(a) Regulated-monopoly profit incentive.** As an investor-owned regulated utility, CEI's authorized rate of return is calculated on rate base; capital investment expands rate base; rate-base growth supports earnings. ## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**
ASSUMPTION: 60 months (approximately 1,800 days) as a hypothetical-only estimate. Dismantling of an investor-owned regulated distribution utility is not a contemplated routine action; the relevant scenarios are (a) acquisition by another utility or by a public entity; (b) bankruptcy reorganization (FirstEnergy Solutions, the previously affiliated competitive generation entity, did go through bankruptcy in 2018-2020 and emerged as Energy Harbor); (c) municipalization (purchase by one or more municipalities, with PUCO and federal regulatory approval); (d) restructuring under federal or state corrective-action order. **9.2 — Dismantling process:**
- **Months 1–12:** PUCO and FERC authorization for restructuring or successor designation; FirstEnergy Board action; identification of successor (acquiring utility, municipal entity, or restructured arrangement); inventory of assets, obligations, customer relationships, and contracts.
- **Months 12–30:** Service transition with operational continuity — distribution service cannot be interrupted; transfer of customer accounts, easements, franchise rights, regulatory certifications, and operational personnel.
- **Months 30–48:** Capital structure restructuring; bond defeasance or transfer; pension and OPEB obligation transfer; vendor-contract transition.
- **Months 48–60:** Final regulatory closeout; SEC reporting closeout for FirstEnergy if CEI dissolution affects parent reporting; records-retention archival; final litigation closeout.

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- **Distribution infrastructure** — physical assets with depreciation lifetimes of 20-40 years; embedded in built environment.
- **Service-territory certification** — PUCO certificate of public convenience and necessity is a long-duration arrangement.
- **Easements and rights-of-way** — typically permanent or long-duration property encumbrances.
- **Municipal franchise agreements** — multi-decade arrangements with dozens of municipalities.
- **Customer-account relationships** — approximately 745,000 accounts requiring service continuity.
- **Pension and OPEB obligations** — multi-decade promises to current and retired employees.
- **Long-term debt obligations** — FirstEnergy bond instruments.
- **PJM membership and operational arrangements** — regional transmission organization participation.
- **Ohio statutory framework** — the regulated-monopoly distribution structure is itself the entrenchment, requiring legislative action to change.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**
- **Personal data (customer accounts, consumption history, billing history, payment history, medical certifications):** Transferred to successor under privacy-compliant arrangements; subject to retention requirements under PUCO rules and applicable law; medical certifications particularly require careful handling under HIPAA-related considerations.
- **Institutional records (Board minutes, financial records, regulatory submissions, corporate-separation audit records):** Archived to successor.

**9.5 — Downstream effect reversibility:**
CEI outputs propagated to other systems include: customer payment histories to credit bureaus; medical-certification records for medically dependent customers; line-extension and easement records to property records; rate-case decisions creating precedent for future cases; corporate-separation audit findings; political-activity reporting; HB 6 enforcement records.
