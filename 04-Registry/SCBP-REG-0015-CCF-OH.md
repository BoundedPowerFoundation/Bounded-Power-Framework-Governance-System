## SECTION 1 — What is this authority?

**1.1 — Mechanism name:** Cleveland Clinic Foundation, doing business as Cleveland Clinic Health System (also referenced as Cleveland Clinic, the Clinic, or CCF). This record covers the Cleveland Clinic Health System as a single integrated authority surface, recognizing that the legal structure includes the Foundation as parent of multiple affiliated tax-exempt and taxable entities consolidated under generally accepted accounting principles.

**1.2 — Functional description:**
Cleveland Clinic is an Ohio nonprofit corporation operating as a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt multispecialty academic medical center founded in 1921. The Health System provides:
- **Acute hospital care** at 23 hospitals (15 in Northeast Ohio, 5 in Florida, 1 in London, and 2 international affiliated facilities) with approximately 6,725 staffed beds across the System and 5,500 in the Ohio core.
- **Tertiary and quaternary clinical services** including organ transplantation (heart, lung, kidney, liver, intestine, face), complex cardiac and vascular surgery, neurosurgery, oncology, and other high-acuity service lines for which Cleveland Clinic functions as a regional and national referral center.
- **Ambulatory care** through 300 outpatient facilities including family health centers, ambulatory surgery centers, specialty clinics, and physician offices.
- **Emergency services** subject to the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA) at hospital emergency departments.
- **Pre-hospital and inter-facility transport** through Cleveland Clinic-affiliated ambulance services and Critical Care Transport.
- **Graduate medical education** as a major teaching institution, with residency and fellowship programs across more than 140 specialties; sponsor of the Cleveland Clinic Lerner College of Medicine in affiliation with Case Western Reserve University School of Medicine.
- **Research** through the Lerner Research Institute, the Cleveland Clinic Innovation District, and clinical-trials infrastructure.
- **Population health and managed care** including participation in Medicare Accountable Care Organization arrangements, Medicare Advantage plans, and commercial-insurance value-based contracts.
- **Management services** for Ashtabula County Medical Center (Ohio) and Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi (UAE).
- **Community health programs** under 501(r) ACA requirements including financial assistance policy administration, community health needs assessments, and community benefit reporting on Form 990 Schedule H.

The System employs approximately 83,000 caregivers worldwide, including approximately 6,600 salaried physicians and researchers and 21,900 registered nurses and advanced practice providers. Operating revenue was approximately $18 billion in 2025 (approximately $15.94 billion in 2024). The System manages an investment portfolio with cash, investments, and endowment reserves measured in the multiple billions of dollars and issues tax-exempt hospital revenue bonds for capital financing.

**3.2 — Coercive ceiling:**
The strongest actions Cleveland Clinic can take or initiate against a person, organization, or premises:
- **Involuntary medical intervention without contemporaneous consent**, when authorized by exigent circumstance, court order, statutory implied consent, or surrogate decision. - **Listing and de-listing decisions for transplant candidates** — a uniquely consequential authority given the federally regulated organ-allocation system in which the Clinic's listing decisions effectively gate access to lifesaving treatment.
- **Refusal of non-emergency care** based on payment, insurance acceptance, or other operational considerations, subject to EMTALA's non-discrimination floor and the financial-assistance policy required under IRC §501(r).
- **Credentialing and peer-review action** affecting physicians' practice rights at Clinic facilities; National Practitioner Data Bank reporting that propagates to other facilities and to medical-licensure boards (R63 and R65 attach).
- **Hospital-acquired-condition cost imposition** through billing for care including for events that arguably arose from System-caused harm, subject to CMS hospital-acquired-condition non-payment policies for specified events.
- **Collection action** against patients for balances after insurance, subject to IRC §501(r)(6) limitations on extraordinary collection actions and the institutional financial-assistance policy.
- **Mandatory reporting** of suspected child abuse, elder abuse, communicable disease, and gunshot/stabbing wounds under state and federal law.
- **Personnel action** against approximately 83,000 employees worldwide including termination, discipline, mandatory training, and post-termination non-compete enforcement (subject to applicable state law).
- **Research-subject enrollment** under IRB-approved protocols, with informed-consent procedures bounded by federal Common Rule (45 CFR Part 46) and FDA IND/IDE rules.

Cleveland Clinic does not exercise law-enforcement arrest authority. Cleveland Clinic does not directly impose criminal penalties. Cleveland Clinic does not directly suspend physician medical licenses (that is the Ohio State Medical Board's authority), though its peer-review and NPDB reporting materially influence licensure outcomes.

## SECTION 4 — What harms can it cause?

**4.1 — Bodily harm threshold:**
For a large academic medical system, a substantial portion of authorized clinical activity carries known adverse-event risk. The framework distinguishes normal-and-expected adverse effects (chemotherapy side effects, surgical recovery, anesthesia risk, transplant rejection) from excess harm. Thresholds target excess.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the System's rate of hospital-acquired conditions (HACs) — including central-line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI), catheter-associated urinary tract infections (CAUTI), surgical site infections (SSI), and falls with injury — exceeds CMS-published peer benchmarks for comparable academic medical centers by more than 20%, an internal-and-external structural review of clinical practice in the implicated category is initiated.

Threshold: If, in any rolling 12-month period, the System's risk-adjusted mortality (for AHRQ-defined inpatient-quality-indicator conditions and for major surgical procedures) exceeds peer-benchmark expected mortality by more than 1 standard deviation, the affected service line is reviewed.

Threshold: If a CMS "immediate jeopardy" finding is made against any Cleveland Clinic facility, the finding and its remediation are publicly disclosed under §11 within 30 days of the underlying citation. ANNOTATION: Between 2010 and 2013, CMS investigations into the Cleveland Clinic Foundation resulted in immediate-jeopardy citations; the System was on payment-termination track for 19 months until corrective action was completed. This threshold formalizes that real, recurring possibility.

Threshold: If the System's seven-day, thirty-day, and ninety-day post-discharge mortality and readmission rates for high-risk conditions exceed risk-adjusted peer benchmarks by more than 1 standard deviation in any rolling 24-month period, the discharge-planning protocol is reviewed.

Threshold: If a sentinel event under Joint Commission definition occurs and is not reported, reviewed via root cause analysis, and remediated within the time frames specified in System policy, the lapse is reported under §11.

## SECTION 5 — What stops it?

**STOP PATH 1 — Federal and state regulatory authority; private litigation.**
- Who can activate it: CMS (through Conditions of Participation enforcement, immediate-jeopardy citations, payment-termination threats, exclusion from federal-program participation); HHS Office for Civil Rights (HIPAA enforcement, civil-rights enforcement under §1557 of the ACA); HHS Office of Inspector General (False Claims Act enforcement, exclusion-list authority); the Ohio Department of Health (hospital licensure suspension or revocation under ORC Chapter 3727 and OAC Chapter 3701-13); the Ohio State Medical Board (physician-licensure actions reaching the System through employed physicians); the U.S. Department of Justice (False Claims Act, antitrust, civil-rights enforcement); the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (controlled-substance registration); FDA (research and clinical-trial enforcement); the Joint Commission (accreditation suspension or revocation); private patients (medical malpractice, breach-of-contract, billing disputes); patient advocates (HHS-OCR complaints, NPDB reports); employees (Title VII, ADA, OSHA, whistleblower); state attorneys general (consumer-protection and nonprofit-oversight authority under ORC Chapter 109 and Chapter 1716).
- Trigger condition: Threshold-based (regulatory inspection cycles; mandatory reporting; statutory triggers) and complaint-driven.
- What "stopped" means: Specific findings and corrective-action plans; conditional accreditation; payment termination from Medicare/Medicaid (the most consequential single threat to System operations, as Medicare/Medicaid receipts are a substantial portion of revenue); license revocation for individual facilities; civil monetary penalties; debarment of specified individuals from federal-program participation; jury verdicts in malpractice; settlement-and-consent-decree arrangements with regulators.
- Independence justification: Federal agencies and the Ohio government are structurally separate from the System. The Joint Commission is an independent accrediting body. Courts are constitutionally separate from healthcare-provider governance.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Administrative-procedure-act process for regulatory matters; ordinary appellate process for litigation; Joint Commission appeal process for accreditation.

**STOP PATH 2 — Board of Trustees / Board of Governors governance.**
- Who can activate it: The Cleveland Clinic Foundation Board of Trustees (the principal fiduciary body); the Board of Governors (which under historic Cleveland Clinic governance has held physician-self-governance authority over the medical staff and clinical operations); the audit-and-compliance committee of the Board.
- Trigger condition: Fiduciary review cycles; risk-assessment processes; mandatory reporting from internal audit and compliance functions; whistleblower complaints to the Board's audit-and-compliance committee.
- What "stopped" means: Executive removal or replacement (the CEO serves at the Board's pleasure); strategy redirection; policy change at any level; program discontinuation; capital-plan modification.
- Independence justification: Trustees are not employed by the System and are subject to fiduciary duty under Ohio nonprofit-corporation law. The compliance-and-audit committee structure under Sarbanes-Oxley-influenced nonprofit governance norms creates a reporting channel structurally independent of executive management. ANNOTATION: Board independence is a relative concept; trustees are typically Clinic stakeholders by professional and philanthropic affiliation, and the framework concern is whether the Board's posture is genuinely independent of executive leadership or principally ratifying.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Ordinary corporate-governance process.

**STOP PATH 3 — Market-and-mission discipline.**
- Who can activate it: Patients who choose another provider (where alternatives exist); payers (insurance companies, employer purchasers) who exclude Cleveland Clinic from their networks or who negotiate constraining contract terms; competing providers (University Hospitals; MetroHealth System; the for-profit Cleveland-area hospitals; regional academic medical centers); philanthropic donors who redirect giving; bond investors who increase the System's cost of capital in response to financial-or-clinical-performance concerns; the public, through reputational dynamics following publicly reported incidents.
- Trigger condition: Market processes operating continuously; episodic events (publicly reported safety incidents; regulatory findings; financial-performance concerns).
- What "stopped" means: Market-share reduction; insurance-network exclusion; payment-rate constraint in commercial-payer negotiations; access-to-capital constraint; philanthropic-revenue reduction; reputational consequences affecting recruitment of physicians, researchers, and patients.
- Independence justification: Patients, payers, donors, and bond investors are not part of the System's institutional structure.
- Post-stop review process and timeline: Market processes operate on their own timeline; there is no formal review mechanism.

## SECTION 9 — How does it end?

**9.1 — Estimated dismantling time:**
ASSUMPTION: 84 months (approximately 2,520 days, or seven years) for full wind-down of the consolidated Cleveland Clinic Health System. Justification: Cleveland Clinic is the largest authority in this registry sequence by every operational metric — patient volume, revenue, employees, capital footprint, multi-jurisdictional regulatory complexity. Dismantling would require (a) statutory and regulatory authorization across multiple jurisdictions; (b) transition of approximately 16 million annual patient encounters and ongoing clinical relationships including transplant programs, oncology continuity-of-care, and other longitudinal-care obligations; (c) successor-provider designation for each service line in each market; (d) transition of approximately 83,000 employees; (e) disposition of approximately $18 billion in annual operating activity and multi-billion-dollar capital base; (f) closeout of tax-exempt hospital revenue bond obligations; (g) transition of the Lerner College of Medicine and graduate medical education programs; (h) transition of research portfolios including federal grants and clinical trials; (i) records continuity for permanent medical records; (j) Medicare and Medicaid participation transitions; (k) international-operations wind-down. The 84-month estimate is conservative and assumes orderly transition rather than crisis-driven dissolution.

**9.2 — Dismantling process:**
- **Months 1–12:** Board-of-Trustees authorization; regulatory engagement with CMS, ODH, the State Attorney General (which under ORC Chapter 109 supervises charitable-trust assets), the IRS (regarding charitable-asset disposition under §501(c)(3) dissolution requirements), the international regulators; successor-provider identification (regional academic medical centers; for-profit hospital chains; new entities to be formed); inventory of clinical relationships, contracts, bond obligations, and records.
- **Months 12–36:** Service-line transitions sequenced by criticality — transplant programs and oncology first; ongoing inpatient care; ambulatory services; emergency services; education and research programs.
- **Months 36–60:** Personnel transition under collective-bargaining and applicable employment-contract terms; real-estate disposition; international-operations wind-down.
- **Months 60–84:** Bond-defeasance or transfer to successor; final regulatory closeout; charitable-asset disposition under ORC §1702.49 and federal 501(c)(3) requirements (residual assets must go to other 501(c)(3) entities); records-management transition with permanent medical records transferred to successor or to designated archival entity under HIPAA-compliant arrangement.

**9.3 — Entrenchment factors:**
- **Built infrastructure and master-plan commitments.** Multi-decade physical investments.
- **Long-term debt obligations.** Tax-exempt hospital revenue bonds in multiple series with maturity dates extending through the 2040s and beyond.
- **Pension and OPEB obligations.** Multi-decade promises to current and retired employees.
- **Longitudinal patient relationships** — transplant recipients with lifetime relationships to the System; oncology patients in active treatment; pediatric patients with chronic conditions; patients receiving complex specialty care.
- **Research portfolios** — federal grants with multi-year performance periods; clinical trials with active enrollment.
- **Educational commitments** — residency programs with multi-year training cycles; medical students mid-curriculum.
- **Regional economic dependence** — Cleveland Clinic is one of the largest employers in Northeast Ohio; community economic structure is partially dependent on continued operation.
- **National referral patterns** — the System receives referrals from across the United States and internationally; transition would affect care access far beyond the System's direct service area.

**9.4 — Data disposition:**
- **Personal data (medical records, research data, employee records):** Transferred to successor under HIPAA-compliant arrangements; subject to ORC retention schedules (Ohio Revised Code requires hospitals to retain medical records for at least 25 years for adults under ORC §3701.74); research data transferred consistent with grant-and-consent terms.
- **Institutional records (Board minutes, financial records, regulatory submissions, accreditation files):** Archived to successor or to charitable-asset successor under records-management requirements.
- **Derived outputs (credentialing decisions, peer-review records, NPDB reports, research publications, intellectual-property assets):** Credentialing and peer-review records survive any dissolution and may be transferred to successor; NPDB entries are permanent in the federal database; research publications are permanent in the academic record; patent and IP assets transfer under §501(c)(3) charitable-asset rules.
- **Audit logs (clinical-system audit trails, financial audit trails):** Retained per applicable schedules; preserved for any pending litigation or regulatory matter.

**9.5 — Downstream effect reversibility:**
Cleveland Clinic outputs propagated to other systems include: medical records propagated to other providers and insurers; NPDB reports on physicians; research publications and patents propagated to scientific literature and licensee organizations; clinical-pathway and guideline content propagated through specialty society publication and continuing-medical-education; Form 990 and financial-disclosure data propagated to bond markets, research databases, and policy analysis. On dissolution, none of these is automatically invalidated; reversal of individual entries requires individual administrative or judicial action; aggregate effects on regional and national medical practice are not reversed by Clinic dissolution.

**9.6 — Replacement feasibility:**
The Northeast Ohio market includes other academic and community providers (University Hospitals; MetroHealth; St. Vincent Charity; community hospitals) and could be served by some combination of expanded existing-provider capacity and successor entities. The tertiary and quaternary service lines for which Cleveland Clinic is a regional and national referral center would be more difficult to replace; some services (specific transplant programs, specific complex-cardiac procedures, specific rare-disease care) might not be available in Northeast Ohio at the current scale without Clinic. Replacement is feasible but operationally and economically complex.

The declared 84-month timeline is estimated, not verified. ANNOTATION: Cleveland Clinic has not, in publicly available materials reviewed, rehearsed dissolution. Continuity-of-operations planning for individual facilities and service lines exists for emergency-and-disaster scenarios; full-system dissolution planning is not contemplated. Per R43, the §9.1 declaration is treated as estimated.
